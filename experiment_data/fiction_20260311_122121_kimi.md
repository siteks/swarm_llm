# The Forged Skies of Vespera

## Part I: The Weight of Glass

The rain in Vespera didn't fall so much as it dissolved, a slow erosion that turned the city's marble facades into weeping monuments and its citizens into ghosts of themselves. Kaela Vance watched it streak the windows of her third-floor studio, each droplet carving a path through the grime, revealing brief glimpses of the world outside—a world she had spent seven years trying to forget.

The object on her worktable pulsed with a faint, sickly light, like a dying star captured in amber. It was a pocket watch, gold plating worn thin by generations of anxious thumbs, its glass face cracked in a spiderweb pattern. Inside, time had stopped at precisely 11:47. Kaela didn't need to know why. The client had paid in cash, heavy coins that clinked like bones, and that was sufficient.

She rolled up the sleeves of her worn shirt, revealing forearms laced with silver scars that caught the gaslight. Each mark was a memory—not hers, but fragments of others she had absorbed through carelessness in her early years. The smooth line across her left wrist was a baker's memory of kneading dough. The jagged pattern on her right forearm was a soldier's last stand. She had learned to wear gloves since then.

"Are you going to stare at it all day, or are you going to work?"

Mira perched on the windowsill, one scrawny leg dangling into the void, the other drawn up to her chest. The girl was eleven, maybe twelve—age was a flexible concept in the Lower Wards—and had attached herself to Kaela like a burr six months ago. She had the sharp face of a street cat and the same wary grace.

"I'm contemplating," Kaela said, her voice rough with disuse. "It's a crucial part of the process."

"Contemplating is what rich folks do while servants bring them tea. You? You're stalling."

Kaela's mouth twitched. The child wasn't wrong. She turned from the window and regarded the watch again. The client—a cadaverous man in a suit that smelled of mothballs—had been specific. "Make it remember peace," he'd whispered, sliding the watch across the table. "Make it remember a moment when the world was not ending."

Vague requests were dangerous. Peace could mean a thousand things: the hush before battle, the silence between lovers, the quiet of a grave. But the coins had been heavy, and Kaela's rent was three weeks overdue. The landlord, a practical man, had begun leaving lengthier notes that frequently mentioned the window and the pavement.

She lifted the watch, feeling its history hum against her skin. It remembered train journeys and formal dinners. It remembered a son's graduation. It remembered being clutched in a dying man's hand as he bled out on a factory floor—the moment when time had stopped for him, and thus for the watch.

Kaela closed her eyes and reached for the forge.

The power came not from her hands but from the hollow space behind her ribs, where her own memories lived like birds in a cage. She had been twelve when she first felt it—a tingling in her fingertips when she held her mother's locket, the sudden overwhelming scent of lavender and grief that didn't belong to her. It had taken years to understand that she could not just read memories but write them, imprint them onto objects like a seal in wax.

Now, she gathered the sensation of peace from her own meager store. She remembered the morning after the Great Strike, when the factories had fallen silent for the first time in decades and the air was clean. She remembered standing on the roof of this very building, Mira's small hand in hers, watching the twin suns of Vespera set in violet and gold. She remembered—gently, carefully—the feeling that for one moment, the city's endless churn toward self-destruction had paused.

She wove these threads into the watch, nursing the cracked glass with her thumbs. The metal warmed, then cooled. The pulse steadied, became a quiet heartbeat. When she opened her eyes, the spiderweb crack had sealed itself, not gone but... accepted, like a scar that had finally healed.

Mira leaned forward, eyes wide. "Did it work?"

Kaela set the watch down. "As well as it ever does."

A knock at the door—three sharp raps, then two slow. Kaela's spine straightened. That was the code. She gestured for Mira to hide. The girl melted into the shadows behind a stack of broken mirrors like water into stone.

Kaela opened the door a crack. Elder Cassian stood there, leaning heavily on his cane, his face a topographical map of a life spent fighting losing battles. He had been her teacher, once. Now he was just another ghost.

"You're forging again," he said. Not a question.

"I have to eat."

"You have to stay hidden." He pushed inside without invitation, his limp severe today. The damp had gotten into his bones. "They know, Kaela. The Sentinels have your description. Someone talked."

Kaela shut the door, her throat tightening. "Who?"

"Does it matter? Could have been any of them. You were never as careful as you thought." Cassian lowered himself into the room's only chair, a rickety affair that groaned under his weight. "They say you're planning something. A forgery so vast it could topple the Ministry."

"I don't even vote, Cassian. You know that."

"I know what you are. That's enough for them to be afraid."

Seven years ago, Kaela had been the Ministry's most valuable asset—a weapon disguised as an artisan. She had forged memories into the very stones of the Grand Archive, rewriting the history of the Civil War to favor the victors. She had made the dead remember surrender. She had made the living forget their murdered children. She had been so good at it that she had nearly convinced herself it was art, not atrocity.

When she had finally broken, when the weight of all that fabricated history had become too much, Cassian had helped her vanish into the Lower Wards. He had taught her how to be small, how to be nobody.

"They're afraid of memories," Kaela said, turning back to the window. The rain had intensified, turning the street below into a river of oil and filth. "They're right to be."

Cassian followed her gaze. "The Chimera bombed the Foundry yesterday. Twelve dead. The Ministry's calling it an act of war."

"Isn't it always?"

"The difference is, this time they might be right." He leaned forward, his voice dropping. "The Chimera isn't just attacking buildings, Kaela. They're attacking history. They've started destroying Archive artifacts. If they get enough of them—if they erase enough of the past—"

"The Ministry loses its legitimacy." Kaela finished. "The foundation of their power is that they control memory. They control what we remember, so they control what we believe."

"And you could rebuild it," Cassian said softly. "You could forge new artifacts, restore what was lost."

Kaela spun on him, fury rising hot and sharp. "I left that life!"

"You left because you were haunted. But the ghosts you're running from are the same ones everyone in this city carries." Cassian stood, pain creasing his face. "I'm not asking you to serve the Ministry. I'm asking you to consider serving something bigger than your own guilt."

He left a card on the table—a thick, cream-colored thing embossed with a name: LORD ALRIC VORENTHAL. Below it, in precise copperplate: *Matters of Historical Preservation.*

Then he was gone, swallowed by the rain and the city's indifferent shadows.

Mira reemerged, picking up the card. "Lord," she said, tasting the word like a foreign fruit. "That means money."

"It means trouble."

"He's from the Spire." She held the card up to the light. "They don't come down here unless they want something that's theirs or something they think should be."

Kaela took the card, running her thumb over the embossed letters. The paper remembered being pressed, being written upon. It remembered the scent of expensive ink and the haste of a secretary's hand. It remembered pride.

She dropped it in the bin. "I'm not for sale."

But that night, as the rain turned to sleet and the landlord's latest note was wedged under her door (this one included a helpful diagram), Kaela fished the card from the trash. Lord Alric Vorenthal. Matters of Historical Preservation.

She had a feeling he didn't want to preserve anything. He wanted to create something that had never existed.

The problem was, she was the only one who could tell the difference.

## Part II: The Architect of Ghosts

Lord Alric Vorenthal's residence occupied the fourteenth floor of the Vorenthal Tower, one of the seven spires that pierced Vespera's perpetual twilight. The elevator attendant wouldn't look at Kaela, though he visibly recoiled from the scent of cheap soap and poverty that clung to her coat. Mira had insisted on coming, arguing that "rich folks always have better stuff to steal when someone's there to distract them." Kaela had reluctantly agreed, if only to keep the girl from burning down the studio in her absence.

The doors opened onto a world of amber light and whispers. The floors were marble so white they seemed to glow from within. The walls were lined with portraits whose eyes followed Kaela's progress, each canvas saturated with memory so dense she could taste it—old money, old blood, old secrets. She kept her gloves on, her fists clenched.

Lord Alric waited in a solarium that overlooked the city. He was younger than she expected, perhaps thirty-five, with the kind of beauty that seemed carved rather than born. His features were sharp enough to cut, his eyes the color of winter ice. He stood with his hands clasped behind his back, studying the smog-choked skyline as if it were a painting he might purchase.

"Miss Vance," he said without turning. "Thank you for coming."

"I didn't realize I had a choice."

"You always have a choice." He gestured to a chair. "Please."

Kaela remained standing. Mira had vanished into the corridors, silent as smoke. "Elder Cassian said you had a proposition."

"A job, yes." Alric finally turned, and his gaze was like a scalpel. "I require a memory forged into an object of considerable significance. The compensation would be... substantial."

"I don't work for the Ministry."

"I am not the Ministry." A shadow crossed his face. "My sister is the curator of the Vorenthal Collection. Three nights ago, it was broken into. They took only one thing: a ceremonial dagger from the Age of Unification. Are you familiar with it?"

Kaela stiffened. Every schoolchild knew the story—the dagger that had ended the civil war, the blade that had severed the head of the tyrant-king Verik and united the seven provinces under one rule. It was a symbol. More than that, it was a memory anchor, its very existence proof of the Ministry's divine right.

"The Chimera?" she asked.

"Undoubtedly. And they will destroy it. Not for the gold, not for the jewels in its hilt, but for what it represents. They will melt it down and erase the memory of unity itself." Alric's voice held no emotion, but his knuckles were white. "I want you to forge a copy. A perfect copy, with the memory intact. They will have their symbol; we will have ours."

Kaela laughed, a bitter sound. "You want me to counterfeit history."

"I want you to preserve it. The Ministry's Archive is compromised. Their memories are failing—they were always weak, always subject to revision. But a forge-work from you... that would be real. As real as such things can be." He stepped closer, and Kaela caught the scent of old books and gunpowder. "You were the best. You could make a stone remember being a mountain. You could make a corpse remember its mother’s lullaby."

"I was a weapon."

"You were an artist." His voice dropped. "And artists don't destroy their work. They perfect it."

He offered her a box. Inside, on velvet blacker than the space between stars, lay the dagger. Or what remained of it. The blade was intact, etched with runes that still glowed faintly. But the hilt was shattered, the jewels pried from their sockets like eyes gouged from a corpse. It remembered violence, remembered betrayal. It remembered the hot spray of blood and the cold satisfaction of justice.

Kaela's hands itched to touch it without gloves, to feel the full weight of its history. She kept them on. "You want me to fix it?"

"I want you to reforge it. Not as it was—a tool of execution—but as it should have been. A symbol of unity, of sacrifice, of peace." Alric's eyes were fever-bright. "Make it remember mercy."

The request was impossible. You couldn't change a memory, only amplify or suppress aspects of it. Mercy had no place in this object's history. To add it would be pure fabrication.

It would be a lie.

But the coins Alric placed on the table—more than she could earn in five years, enough to get out of Vespera entirely, enough to buy Mira a future—those were very real.

"I'll need a month," she said.

"You have two weeks. The peace summit is in sixteen days. The delegates need to see the dagger, to touch it, to *remember* what we're fighting for." Alric smiled, and it didn't reach his eyes. "Elder Cassian assured me you were a woman of principle. I trust those principles can be flexible."

Kaela took the box. The dagger felt cold even through the velvet, cold and hungry.

As the elevator descended, Mira materialized at her side, pockets bulging with who-knew-what treasures. "He smells like trouble," she announced.

"That's cologne."

"Same thing." Mira peered into the box. "That thing's broken."

"It will be."

"Are you gonna fix it?"

Kaela looked at the girl's face, at the thinness of her cheeks, the sharpness of her hope. "I'm going to try."

That night, Kaela dreamed of the forge for the first time in years. Not the gentle art of imprinting memories, but the military forge: the Ministry's version, where memory was a weapon, where history was ductile metal to be hammered into any shape they desired. She dreamed of the Archives, those endless corridors where objects were stored like bottled lightning, each one a lie they had told so often it had become truth.

She woke with the taste of iron in her mouth and a single thought: *Alric doesn't want to preserve history. He wants to replace it.*

The dagger on her worktable seemed to pulse in agreement.

## Part III: The Traitor's Truth

The work consumed her. Days bled into nights as Kaela studied the dagger, mapping its memories like a surgeon mapping veins. She used her tools—silver needles, threads of spider-silk, vials of distilled emotion—to tease apart the layers of violence and find what lay beneath.

There was something strange. The memory of the execution was there, vivid and brutal, but it was... shallow. A performance. The blade remembered striking Verik's neck, remembered the cheers of the crowd, but the *weight* of it was wrong. It remembered like a story told too many times, details worn smooth by repetition.

On the ninth day, she found it. A hidden seam in the memory, buried so deep it was almost part of the metal itself. The blade's true memory: it had not killed Verik. It had killed a scapegoat, a body double. The real tyrant had died years earlier, smothered in his sleep by his own ministers. The public execution had been staged. The unity had been built on a lie from the very first moment.

The dagger remembered being part of a conspiracy. It remembered the secret, and it remembered the weight of that secret—the way it had poisoned everything that came after.

She dropped it. The blade clattered against the wood, and the sound was too loud in her small room. Mira looked up from where she was repairing a pair of stolen gloves, her eyes sharp.

"You found something."

Kaela couldn't speak. Her throat had closed around the truth, a bone she couldn't swallow. She had spent seven years trying to atone for the lies she'd helped the Ministry tell—smoothing over massacres, erasing dissent, turning revolutionaries into traitors through the quiet magic of false memory. And now this: the founding myth of Vespera itself, the moment of unity that justified every cruelty the Ministry had inflicted since, was a stage play. A forgery more elaborate than anything she could have conceived.

The tyrant-king hadn't died by the blade. He'd died in his bed, probably pleading, probably soiled with fear, murdered by the very men who would later claim his death as their heroic triumph.

The dagger remembered. And now Kaela remembered too.

"We have to leave," she said, the words tasting of ash. "Tonight."

Mira stood, the gloves forgotten. "Why?"

"Because I know something they would kill to keep secret. Because the man who gave me this—" she gestured at the dagger, "—doesn't know what it really is. And when he figures out that I know, he'll—"

The window exploded.

Not shattered—*exploded*. Glass became a thousand knives that hung in the air for a suspended moment before raining down like judgment. Kaela threw herself over Mira, covering the girl's body with her own. Shards bit through her coat, into her shoulders, her scalp. She felt the hot trickle of blood, but no pain. Not yet.

A figure dropped through the empty window frame, landing in a crouch on her worktable. They wore a mask of polished brass, featureless except for two slits for eyes. The Chimera crest—a snake devouring its own tail—was emblazoned on their chest plate. In their hand, they held a device that looked like a cross between a pistol and a tuning fork. It hummed with a frequency that made Kaela's teeth ache.

"Memory thief," the intruder said, their voice modulated, genderless. "You have something that doesn't belong to you."

Kaela scrambled back, pulling Mira with her. The girl was trembling but silent, already calculating exits. There were none. The door was behind the intruder. The window was a death drop.

"The dagger isn't mine," Kaela said, keeping her voice level. "I'm just a craftswoman. I was commissioned—"

"By Lord Alric Vorenthal. We know." The Chimera agent stood, the worktable groaning under their weight. "We know what he wants you to do. We know what the Ministry plans for the peace summit. You are a tool in their hand, Memory Forger. Will you let them use you again?"

The title wasn't an insult. It was a classification. They knew exactly what she was.

Mira's hand found Kaela's. The girl's palm was slick with sweat, but her grip was steady. *The knife*, Mira's fingers spelled against Kaela's wrist. *Your boot.*

Kaela's boot knife. Right. While the agent monologued, while they thought they had her cornered, she could—

"Don't." The agent's head tilted. "We aren't here to kill you. If we were, you'd be dead." They holstered the humming device. "We're here to make you an offer."

Kaela laughed, the sound edged hysteria. "Everyone's making offers tonight. My calendar's full."

"This one comes without coin. Only truth." The agent reached up, unfastened the brass mask. It came away with a hiss of released steam.

Beneath was a woman's face, sharp-boned and severe, with eyes the color of storm clouds. She looked to be in her forties, old enough to have lived through the last years of the war, old enough to remember what the city was before the Ministry's absolute peace.

"My name is Selene Kastor. I was there when they found the dagger in the Archive. I was there when your Lord Vorenthal commissioned its... restoration." She said the last word like a curse. "I saw the original memory. I know what you now know."

Kaela's blood went cold. "How?"

"Because I helped steal it." Selene stepped down from the table, boots crunching on broken glass. "The Chimera doesn't want to destroy the dagger, Forger. That's Ministry propaganda. We want to expose it. We want to show the delegates at the peace summit that their entire foundation is a lie. But we can't do that without a memory forger. The original's memory is degrading. Too many hands, too many years. It needs to be... refreshed. Made whole again."

"So you can what? Replace one lie with another?"

"So we can show them the truth." Selene's voice cracked with urgency. "The peace summit isn't about peace. It's about consolidating power. The Ministry wants to use the dagger's 'memory' of unity to justify annexing the Outer Provinces. To start the war all over again, this time with a flag of righteousness. If they succeed, thousands will die. And you'll have helped them do it."

Mira stepped forward, defiant despite being half Selene's height. "She's not helping anyone. She's leaving."

"Are you?" Selene's gaze was pitiless. "Where will you go? The Ministry has Sentinels at every gate. Vorenthal has watchers on every dock. You've touched the truth, and that makes you dangerous. They won't let you leave with it."

Kaela looked at the dagger, lying where it had fallen. It gleamed in the gaslight, innocent as a snake. She thought of all the objects she had forged in her years with the Ministry, all the truths she had buried beneath layers of comfortable fiction. She had walked away because she couldn't carry the weight anymore. But she had never tried to unmake what she'd done. She'd just... stopped.

"Even if I agreed," she said slowly, "even if I helped you expose it, they'd just kill me. They'd kill her." She squeezed Mira's hand.

"Then we don't expose it at the summit." Selene's smile was a thin, sharp thing. "We expose it before. We show the delegates the real memory—the murder, the conspiracy. We let them come to the summit already knowing. Vorenthal can't manipulate an audience that's seen the truth."

"And how do you propose we do that? March into the Grand Archive and hold a viewing party?"

"Better." Selene pulled a scroll case from her belt, undid the clasp. Inside was a photograph, crisp and recent. It showed a woman in Ministry whites, her face serene, her hands clasped around a velvet box. "Vorenthal's sister. The one who curates the Collection. She's taking the original dagger to the Archive for 'verification' tomorrow night. A secret trip, or so she thinks. We'll intercept her. You'll touch the real blade, copy its memory into this one—" she gestured at Kaela's table, "—and we'll swap them. She'll carry a forgery back to her brother, never knowing the difference."

Kaela studied the photograph. The woman had Alric's eyes, the same winter ice, but where his beauty was carved, hers was soft, almost melancholy. She looked like someone who believed in the stories she was told.

"What's her name?"

"Lady Elara Vorenthal. And before you ask, yes, she's complicit. She knows the Archive's secrets. She helps maintain them." Selene's voice hardened. "Don't let the face fool you. The Ministry's mercy is always delivered by soft hands."

Mira tugged on Kaela's sleeve. "Don't," she whispered. "It's a trap. It has to be."

And it probably was. But Kaela looked at the dagger again, at the lie forged into its very molecules. She thought of all the other lies she'd helped etch into the city's bones. How many times had she told herself she was just surviving? How many times had she let guilt paralyze her instead of guiding her?

"Even if I copy the true memory," she said, "the delegates might not care. Truth is inconvenient. Lias are comfortable."

"But some will care." Selene's voice dropped to something almost human. "Enough to matter. And even if they don't—aren't you tired of being the hand that holds the pen? Don't you want to be the hand that erases, just once?"

Kaela thought of the landlord's notes, the weight of the coins Alric had given her, the scars on her forearms that weren't her own. She thought of Mira, who deserved a city that wasn't built on bones.

"Tell me the plan," she said.

Mira made a sound like a kicked dog, but Kaela didn't look at her. She couldn't. If she saw the girl's disappointment, she might lose her nerve.

Selene's plan was simple, which meant it was probably suicidal. The Archive's under-levels connected to the old sewer system, a labyrinth of tunnels that predated the Spire. A Chimera cell had been mapping them for months. They'd found a route that led to the Archive's private transfer chamber, where Lady Elara would arrive with her escort.

"We'll have twelve minutes," Selene said, sketching the layout on a scrap of Kaela's paper. "The escort is four Sentinels. They'll be focused outward, not inward. We'll create a distraction at the main gates. You slip in, make the touch, slip out."

"That's it? That's the plan?"

"That's the part you're involved in. Trust me, Forger, the rest is more complicated."

Kaela didn't trust her. But she trusted Alric Vorenthal even less.

They left before dawn, slipping through the Lower Wards' shadowed alleys while the city still slept. Selene moved like someone who had memorized every crack in the pavement, every blind spot in the Sentinels' patrols. Mira followed silently, her small hand in Kaela's, her presence both anchor and accusation.

The sewer entrance was hidden behind a collapsed tenement, its mouth breathing damp and decay. Selene lit a phosphor lamp that cast green, sickly light on walls slick with things Kaela didn't want to name.

"You can still turn back," Selene said, not looking at them. "Both of you. No one would blame you."

Mira squeezed Kaela's hand so hard the bones ground together. "We're not turning back."

They walked for what felt like hours through the tunnels, the only sound the trickle of ancient water and the scuttle of rats. Kaela's boots were soon soaked through, and the damp seeped into her bones. She thought about the objects she had forged, the memories she had trapped in metal and glass. She wondered if this was how they felt—buried, forgotten, drowning in the dark.

Finally, Selene stopped at a rusted ladder that disappeared into a stone shaft. "Up there. The transfer chamber is on the other side of that wall. Wait for the signal."

"What signal?" Kaela asked, but Selene was already moving away, swallowed by the dark.

Mira looked up at the ladder. "I can't reach."

"You don't have to." Kaela knelt, her knees cracking. "You're staying here."

"No!" Mira's voice echoed, and Kaela clapped a hand over her mouth.

"Listen to me." She kept her voice low, urgent. "If this goes wrong, you need to be able to run. I can't do what I have to do if I'm worried about you. And if—" she swallowed, "—if I don't come back, Selene will get you out of the city. She owes me that much."

Mira's eyes were huge in the lamplight, filled with a fury that was almost tears. "You promised. You said we'd leave. You said—"

"I know what I said." Kaela's voice broke. "I'm breaking that promise. If you hate me for it, I understand. But one of us has to make it out of this city, Mira. One of us has to see what it's like when the sky isn't made of steel."

She pulled the girl close, pressing her lips to Mira's grimy forehead. "Stay here. Stay quiet. And if you hear fighting, run."

Then she climbed, before Mira could see the tears that were finally, finally falling.

Kaela closed her eyes, reaching for her power. She could feel the dagger's presence on the other side of the wall, a dark star pulling at her senses. Its memory was a knot of lies and violence, a tangled cord. She could unravel it. She knew she could. The question was: could she survive touching it?

The wall was thin. She could probably kick through it. That would be noisy, messy. Or she could wait for Selene's distraction, assuming it ever came, and sneak through whatever door they used.

She was still debating when the gas jet flickered out.

Darkness swallowed the chamber. Kaela froze, her breath loud in her ears. Then she heard it: the scrape of stone on stone, the whisper of fabric, the quiet intake of breath that wasn't hers.

Someone else was in the chamber.

"Lady Elara?" she whispered, though she knew it wasn't her. The presence was too small, too lithe.

A match flared. Mira's face appeared in the flame's halo, defiant and terrified.

Kaela's heart tried to climb out her throat. "Mira, you—"

The wall exploded.

Not a small explosion. Not a controlled demolition. The entire section of brick blew inward with a roar that filled the chamber with dust and debris. Kaela grabbed Mira, pulling her down, covering the girl's body as stones rained down.

Through the dust, she saw figures. Four Sentinels, their faces covered by breathing masks, their rifles raised. And behind them, Lady Elara Vorenthal, staring at the hole in her wall with an expression of utter calm.

"Memory Forger," she said, her voice cutting through the ringing in Kaela's ears. "I was told you might come. Though I confess, I expected someone less... empirical."

Kaela's hands were empty. The dagger was on the other side of the wall, with Elara. Her boot knife was still in her boot, useless against rifles.

"But some stories are too dangerous to be told, even in whispers. The truth of the dagger would not liberate Vespera. It would unmake it."

"Let them have it," Kaela said, her voice raw. "Let it all burn. It's built on bones and lies."

"And what would you build in its place?" Elara's voice was gentle, terrible. "The Chimera? They would tear down the Ministry and replace it with nothing but chaos. No order, no memory, no law. An eternity of revolution, each new regime forgetting the crimes of the last." She knelt—actually knelt—in the rubble, bringing her face level with Kaela's. "You have seen what happens when memory is a weapon. You have seen the Archives, the objects that scream with false lives. Do you want to be the one who fires that weapon again?"

Mira's hand tightened on Kaela's arm. "She's lying. They always lie."

But Kaela saw no lies in Elara's eyes. Only weariness. Only a grief so deep it had become part of her bone structure.

"The Summit," Elara continued. "The delegates from the Outer Provinces—they don't want truth. They want *justice*. They want to know their dead are remembered. They want to know their sacrifices mattered. If we show them the dagger—the *real* dagger, with its memory of staged execution—they will see only that the Ministry is built on fraud. They will see cause for war." She reached out, her soft hand covering Kaela's gloved one. "But if we show them the dagger as it *should* have been—a symbol of sacrifice, of tyranny ended by united action—then we give them a reason to believe in unity again. Not the unity of the past, but the unity of the future."

"A lie for a better tomorrow," Kaela whispered.

"A story to save millions." Elara's thumb traced the leather seam of Kaela's glove. "You are the only one who can tell it. You can forge mercy into this blade. You can give it a memory of peace. Not to hide the truth, but to *transcend* it."

Selene made a strangled noise. "Don't listen to her. She's making you complicit in the same crime you ran from."

"Am I?" Elara stood, turning to face Selene. "Or am I offering her a chance to finish what she started? You wanted to use her gift as a weapon. I want to use it as a shield."

The Sentinel holding Cassian cut his bonds. The old man stumbled forward, falling to his knees before Kaela. "Please," he said, his voice cracking. "Don't make the same mistake I did. I thought I could play both sides. I thought I could keep you safe and serve the truth. But there is no middle ground. Not in this city."

Kaela looked at the pieces of her world, scattered like the brick dust on the floor. Selene, the revolutionary whose certainty was starting to look like fanaticism. Cassian, the broken mentor who had sold her for his family's safety. Mira, the girl who trusted her to be better than the monsters. Elara, the soft-handed curator offering a gentler cage.

And the dagger. Always the dagger.

She stood, pulling Mira up with her. The girl's eyes were bright with tears she refused to shed. "Whatever you choose," Mira said, "I stay with you."

The words broke something in Kaela's chest. She had spent seven years running from responsibility, pretending that smallness was safety. But Mira had never had that luxury. Smallness was just another word for powerlessness.

Kaela turned to Elara. "I won't forge a lie."

Elara's expression flickered. "Then you doom us."

"But I'll forge something else." Kaela took off her gloves. Her bare hands were pale in the gaslight, the silver scars catching the glow like constellations. "The memory of the dagger is fractured. I felt it. It's not just one truth—it's many. The staged execution. The real murder. The weight of the lie. The hope of the people who believed it." She looked at Selene. "You wanted truth. So did you." She looked back at Elara. "But truth isn't a blade you wield. It's a fire you tend."

She stepped forward, holding out her hand. "Give me the dagger. All of you. Let me show you what it really remembers."

The Sentinel looked to Elara, who nodded slowly. The velvet box was placed in Kaela's hands. It was heavier than she expected, the weight of fifty years of history pressing down on her palms.

She opened the box.

The dagger lay there, its runes dark. But when she touched it—when her bare skin finally met the cold metal—it sang. Not a single note, but a chorus. The scapegoat's terror. The conspirators' cold calculation. The crowd's relief. The Ministry's smug triumph. Alric's boyish faith in a story. Elara's weary knowledge. Cassian's complicity. Selene's rage.

And beneath it all, something else. A memory the Ministry had tried to erase but couldn't, because it wasn't in the blade—it was in the space *between* the molecules, the ghost of what had never been recorded.

The memory of the baker whose son had been the double. The woman who had been paid three gold coins to give up her child so the tyrant could live a few more years in fear before his ministers smothered him. The memory of the boy himself, trained to mimic a king's posture, a king's voice, a king's death.

The memory of his last thought, as the blade came down: *At least my family will eat.*

Kaela wept. The tears fell on the blade, sizzling like water on hot iron. She poured her gift into the metal, but not to change it. To *complete* it.

She gave it the memory of the baker's hands kneading dough. The smell of fresh bread. The sound of a child laughing, not in a palace, but in a small apartment in the Lower Wards. She gave it the memory of Cassian's daughter, safe in her bed. She gave it Mira's fierce hope. She gave it Selene's uncompromising rage. She gave it Elara's quiet grief.

She gave it every memory she had that was true, that was human, that was *messy*.

The runes flared, not with the Ministry's cold light, but with a warm, amber glow. The metal heated until it was almost too painful to hold, but Kaela held on. She held on until the scars on her forearms blazed with borrowed lives, until her own memory was a thread in a larger tapestry.

When she finally let go, the dagger was no longer a single object. It was a mirror. It showed whatever truth the holder needed to see.

She placed it back in the box and handed it to Elara. "There. It's done."

Elara opened her mouth, but before she could speak, the chamber rocked with another explosion. This one was distant, reverberating through the tunnels like the city's dying heartbeat.

Selene's communicator crackled. "Sentinels incoming! We have to—"

The Sentinels in the chamber moved as one, their rifles coming up. But they weren't aimed at Kaela or Selene. They were aimed at Elara.

"My lady," the lead Sentinel said, his voice filtered through his mask, "Lord Alric's orders. You're to be detained for conspiring with terrorists."

Elara's face went white. "What?"

"The Lord Governor has declared the peace summit a security risk. He's invoking emergency powers." The Sentinel's rifle didn't waver. "The Ministry is being reorganized. The old guard is being... retired."

Cassian laughed, a wet, broken sound. "You see? You see how they reward loyalty?"

The lead Sentinel shot him. Once, in the chest. The old man crumpled, his blood pooling black on the rubble. Mira screamed.

"Take the girl," the Sentinel ordered. "She's leverage. Kill the rest."

Kaela moved without thinking. She grabbed the velvet box from Elara's frozen hands and flung it at the nearest Sentinel. It struck his mask, and the dagger tumbled out. The moment it touched him, he dropped his rifle, his hands going to his head.

He was seeing something. Something that made him weep.

The other Sentinels hesitated. Selene didn't. She fired her memory-lance. The air rippled, and one Sentinel dropped, his mind erased like a chalkboard.

"Run!" Selene shouted.

Kaela grabbed Mira. Elara's hand found hers, and the three of them ran, leaving the chamber behind, leaving Cassian's body in its spreading stain. They ran through the tunnels while the city above them screamed.

Behind them, the Ministry's peace summit was collapsing before it began. And in Kaela's hand, the dagger pulsed with the weight of every truth it now held.

## Part IV: The Architects of Memory

They emerged in the Gray Market, where the city between the Wards dissolved into a haze of illegal trade and desperate bargains. The rain had stopped, but the air was thick with steam from a thousand cook-fires and the reek of humanity pressed too close together. Kaela's lungs burned. Her hands were shaking.

Mira was silent, her face set in a mask of fury and grief. Elara was trembling, her fine clothes torn and soiled with tunnel-muck. She looked less like a curator of history and more like a refugee from it.

Selene had vanished back into the tunnels, promising to find them a safe house. Kaela didn't believe in safe houses anymore.

"Where do we go?" Elara asked, her voice small.

"Away from here." Kaela pulled them into an alley lined with stalls selling stolen memories in glass vials. Cheap thrills, borrowed courage, someone else's childhood. The irony wasn't lost on her. "Alric will have Sentinels everywhere."

"He's not my brother anymore," Elara whispered. "He was never my brother. He was always the Ministry's."

Kaela stopped, turning on her. "Then who are you?"

Elara met her gaze, and for the first time, there was no artifice, no gentle manipulation. Only exhaustion. "I'm someone who believed that controlling the past could save the future. I'm someone who helped build a prison and called it an archive." She held out her hand. "Give me the dagger."

"Why? So you can destroy it?"

"So I can use it." Elara's fingers trembled. "The delegates are gathering in the Crystal Plaza tomorrow. Alric will present his version of the dagger—the one you were supposed to forge. He'll use it to justify martial law. But if I can show them the real memory, the whole memory..." She trailed off. "No. That would just prove his point. That truth is a weapon."

Kaela looked at the dagger in her hand. Its runes had gone dark again, but she could still feel them humming with the lives she had woven into the metal. "It's not a weapon. It's a choice."

She made her decision then, not in the Ministry's chamber, not in Alric's tower, but here in the Gray Market, where the city's forgotten people bought and sold pieces of each other to survive.

"We're going to the Summit," she said. "But not to give them the dagger. We're going to give them something better."

Mira looked up at her, suspicion warring with hope. "What could be better than that?"

Kaela knelt, putting herself at eye level with the girl. "We're going to give them their own memories back."

The plan was insane. It was impossible. It was exactly the kind of thing that got Memory Forgers killed before they turned twenty.

But Kaela was thirty-three, and she had already died seven years ago when she walked away from the Ministry. Everything since had been borrowed time.

They found a room above a black-market forge, where a one-eyed woman named Jax agreed to rent them space for triple the going rate. Elara's remaining jewelry covered it. The woman had kept a single pearl earring, turning it over in her fingers like a worry stone.

"In the old days," Jax said, lighting a forge that burned with green flame, "we used to forge things that lasted. Now we just forge illusions. Which are you making?"

"Both," Kaela said, and set to work.

She didn't sleep. She barely ate. Jax brought her coffee thick as tar and bread that tasted of ash, and Kaela consumed both without tasting. Her hands moved constantly—her bare hands, because gloves were a luxury she could no longer afford.

Mira helped, her small fingers surprisingly deft at sorting the fragments Kaela needed: vials of emotion traded in the stalls below, each one a stolen moment. Joy. Grief. Fury. Hope. The raw materials of memory.

Elara sat in the corner, the dagger across her lap, talking. Not propaganda, not Ministry lines, but the truth as she had lived it. The pressure from her brother. The knowledge of the Archives' secrets. The way she had soothed her conscience by curating small rebellions—saving this artifact, preserving that truth—while the larger edifice of lies remained.

Kaela listened without judgment. She had no judgment left.

She worked on two objects.

The first was a simple copper bracelet, cheap enough to be overlooked, plain enough to be unremarkable. Into it, she poured the memory of the dagger—not just the memory she had found, but the memory she had made. The baker's boy. The conspirators. The crowd's relief. The cost of peace. She made it a story without heroes, only people making choices and living with them.

The second was smaller. A glass bead, no bigger than a pea. Into it, she poured her own memory of Cassian's death. The crack of the rifle. The wet surprise on his face. The way his blood had looked black in the gaslight. The way his last breath had tasted of regret.

She gave it to Mira.

"Hide this," she said. "If anything happens to me, find a Sentinel. Any Sentinel. Slip it into their pocket. Make them remember."

Mira's eyes widened. "It'll kill them."

"No. It'll make them human."

On the morning of the Summit, the city was locked down. Sentinels lined every street, their rifles gleaming. The Crystal Plaza was a fortress of glass and steel, its walls translucent but impenetrable. The delegates had been arriving for days, housed in secure compounds, their every move watched.

Alric Vorenthal stood at the podium, the velvet box on the lectern before him. He looked younger than his years, his face flushed with triumph. The original dagger—Kaela's forgery, the one she had never meant to make—lay inside, radiating his version of truth.

Kaela watched from the crowd, Mira pressed against her side, Elara disguised in stolen servant's livery. They had gotten

into the plaza by threading through the servant's entrance behind a catering cart piled with gilded platters. Elara had traded her pearl earring for three passes and a promise of silence from a cook who recognized her but didn't care enough for the Ministry to turn her in. The Crystal Plaza was a cauldron of tension, delegates from the Outer Provinces standing in rigid clusters, their bodyguards' hands never far from their weapons. Vespera's elite filled the tiered balconies, their faces serene masks of indifference.

Alric's voice rose above the murmur, amplified by the plaza's acoustics until it seemed to come from the glass itself. "We stand today at a precipice," he declared, his hands resting on the velvet box. "Behind us, the darkness of division. Before us, the light of unity. And guiding us forward, this—" he lifted the lid, "—the blade that severed tyranny from our past."

The dagger caught the morning light, its runes blazing with false glory. The crowd gasped, a collective intake of breath that sounded like prayer. Kaela could feel the memory radiating from it, Alric's version: clean, heroic, uncomplicated. The tyrant's head rolling, the crowd's jubilation, the dawn of a new era. It was a beautiful lie, polished to perfection.

Mira's hand found hers, small and hot. "Now?"

"Wait," Kaela whispered, her eyes scanning the perimeter. Sentinels stood at every exit, their rifles held at ready. In the balconies, sharpshooters tracked the crowd through polished scopes. They were waiting for something—for someone—to make a move.

Alric lifted the dagger higher. "Touch it," he urged the delegates. "Feel the weight of our shared history. Let it remind you that we are one people, one purpose."

A delegate from the Outer Provinces, a woman with gray hair braided in the northern style, stepped forward hesitantly. Her hand trembled as it reached for the blade. Kaela saw the moment her fingers brushed the metal—the flicker of recognition, the way her eyes widened. Alric's forgery was good, but not perfect. It lacked the depth, the resonance of a true memory. The delegate pulled her hand back as if burned.

"It's cold," she said, her voice carrying despite its softness. "Dead."

Alric's smile didn't falter. "History can feel that way to those who weren't there."

"But I was there," the woman said. "I was seven years old. My father held me on his shoulders to see the tyrant's procession. I remember the smell of the crowd—sweat and fear and something else. Something sweet, like rot." She looked at the dagger, then at Alric. "This doesn't remember that."

The plaza held its breath. Kaela felt the moment crystallize, the fracture spreading through the crowd's certainty. This was it. This was the crack where truth could seep in.

She moved.

She didn't run. Running drew eyes. She simply walked, pulling Mira with her, threading through the press of bodies until they were close enough to the podium to see the sweat on Alric's upper lip. Elara melted away, moving toward the balcony stairs. Their plan was a three-pronged distraction, crude but effective.

Kaela palmed the copper bracelet. The memory inside it felt hot enough to burn, heavy with the weight of every life the dagger had touched. She needed to get it close enough to the original to create a resonance, a feedback loop that would broadcast the complete memory to everyone in the plaza. It was a technique the Ministry had taught her, a way to mass-program loyalty during the war. She had never used it on this scale. She wasn't sure she could survive it.

Alric was speaking again, his voice taking on a desperate edge. "Our memories can deceive us, especially from childhood—"

"My memory is clear," the delegate insisted. "The tyrant who died that day had a scar on his left cheek. My father pointed it out. He said, 'Look, child, even monsters bear marks of their making.'" She pointed at the dagger in Alric's hand. "Show me that memory."

Alric's knuckles whitened on the hilt. "This is not a theater for peasant superstitions—"

Kaela threw the bracelet.

It arced through the air, a copper spark catching the light. Alric saw it, his eyes widening. He tried to block it, but he was holding the dagger, and the bracelet was drawn to it like iron to a magnet. It struck the blade with a sound like a bell tolling underwater.

The memory detonated.

Not a flash of light or a burst of sound. It was a wave of pure, unfiltered history that crashed through the plaza, through the glass walls, through the marrow of every person present. Kaela felt it hit her like a physical blow, driving her to her knees. Mira gasped beside her, her eyes rolling back.

They all saw it. The baker's boy, his cheeks hollow with hunger, being fitted for the tyrant's robes. The ministers' whispered plans, their faces in shadow. The real Verik, already dead and cold in his bed. The scapegoat's last breath, the sweetness of rot on his tongue. The crowd's roar, genuine in its relief. The Ministry's architects, watching from a hidden balcony, already planning how to shape this moment into myth.

But they saw more. They saw the baker, years later, receiving her three gold coins, using them to buy a shop that would feed her family for generations. They saw the delegate's father, weeping in private because he had known it was a double and been grateful anyway—because the symbol mattered more than the truth. They saw Cassian, younger, idealistic, forging his first false memory to stop a massacre that would have killed hundreds. They saw Selene, watching her brother die in a Sentinel raid, vowing to burn the whole system down.

They saw Elara, in her tower, polishing artifacts and whispering the real stories to them when no one was listening, building a secret library of truth in her mind because she had no other place to keep it.

And they saw Kaela, seven years old, touching her mother's locket and feeling the grief of a woman she had never met, and understanding for the first time that memory was never just one person's burden.

The wave passed. The plaza was silent except for the sound of weeping. Delegates sat on the glass floor, their faces in their hands. Sentinels lowered their rifles, confusion written in their postures. Even the sharpshooters in the balconies had gone still, their scopes forgotten.

Alric stood alone on the podium, the dagger still raised, but his arm was shaking. He had seen it too. He had felt the weight of every choice that had led to this moment. The bracelet had fused to the blade, copper flowing into the cracks in the hilt like molten gold.

"Is it true?" he whispered, not to the crowd, but to himself.

Kaela stood on trembling legs. Her nose was bleeding. She could feel blood in her ears, too, a warm trickle. The effort had ruptured something inside her. "It's all true," she said, her voice carrying despite its weakness. "And none of it is. That's what you never understood, Alric. History isn't a weapon. It's a wound we keep reopening, hoping this time it will heal."

He looked at her, and for the first time, he saw her. Not a tool, not a forger, but a woman with silver scars on her arms and a girl's hand clutched in hers. "What have you done?"

"I've given them what you couldn't," Kaela said. "A reason to choose."

The Sentinels moved then, but not on Alric's orders. They moved on Elara, who had appeared at the balcony rail. She held something in her hands—a glass bead that caught the light like a tear.

"Mira," Kaela breathed. The girl was gone from her side, slipped away in the chaos. She saw her then, on the balcony behind Elara, her small hand pushing the bead into the pocket of a Sentinel commander.

The effect was immediate. The commander stumbled, his hand going to his head. He saw Cassian's death, not as a necessary action, but as a murder. He saw his own hands on the rifle. He saw the old man's surprise, the way his body had folded to the ground. He remembered pulling the trigger, and he remembered wanting to throw the weapon into the river immediately after.

He turned his rifle on his own men. "Stand down!" he shouted, his voice raw. "All Sentinels, stand down!"

Chaos erupted. Some Sentinels obeyed. Others didn't. A shot rang out, then another. The delegates scrambled for cover. Kaela pulled Mira down, shielding her as the plaza became a battlefield.

But it wasn't the battle anyone had expected. It wasn't Ministry versus Chimera, or Vespera versus the Outer Provinces. It was a hundred individual battles, each Sentinel and delegate and citizen fighting the memory of what they had just seen, what they had just *felt*.

Alric stood untouched on the podium, the dagger still in his hand. He looked down at it, then at Kaela. "Can you fix it?" he asked, and for the first time, he sounded like a man asking for help, not a lord giving orders.

Kaela stood, pulling Mira with her. The girl's face was streaked with tears and blood, but her eyes were clear. "No," Kaela said. "But we can build something that doesn't need fixing."

She held out her hand. Alric looked at it, then at the dagger. He seemed to weigh something invisible, something heavier than gold or steel. Then he placed the dagger in her palm.

The moment he let go, the runes flared one last time, so bright they left afterimages on Kaela's vision. The memory had stabilized, integrated. It was no longer a weapon or a symbol. It was simply a record, complete and unchangeable.

A shot cracked through the plaza. Alric jerked, a red bloom appearing on his chest. He looked down at it with mild surprise, as if noticing a wine stain on his waistcoat. Then he folded, falling from the podium onto the glass floor.

Kaela spun, searching for the shooter. She saw him in the highest balcony—a Sentinel, his face obscured, his rifle still smoking. He was aiming at her next.

Mira moved faster. She snatched the dagger from Kaela's hand and threw it. Not as a weapon, but as a message. It spun through the air, end over end, the memory inside it broadcasting as it flew. The Sentinel saw it coming, saw the truth in it, and lowered his rifle. He didn't try to catch the blade. He let it fall, clattering against the balcony rail.

He put the rifle to his own head.

"No!" Kaela shouted, but the shot had already fired. His body crumpled, falling from the balcony like a dropped puppet.

Silence returned, deeper this time. The fighting had stopped. The plaza was littered with bodies—Alric, the two Sentinels, a delegate who had been trampled in the panic. But most were alive, gasping, weeping, *remembering*.

Elara descended from the balcony, her servant's disguise incongruous with the horror on her face. She knelt beside her brother's body, her hand hovering over his wound. "He thought he could control it," she said softly. "He thought if he had the right story, he could make everyone believe."

"He almost did," Kaela said.

Elara looked up at her, tears cutting clean paths through the grime on her cheeks. "What happens now?"

Now. The word hung in the air like a held breath. The Ministry would not fall in a day. The Outer Provinces would not suddenly embrace Vespera. The Chimera would not lay down their bombs. The city was still a cage, and they were still inside it.

But the lock had been picked.

Kaela helped Mira to her feet, steadying the girl when she swayed. The memory wave had hit her harder, her young mind less able to filter the overload. "Now," Kaela said, "we let them remember. All of them."

She walked to the podium, Mira's hand in hers, the dagger resting in her other palm. The delegates watched her approach, their faces raw. She set the dagger on the lectern, not as a symbol, but as an offering.

"This is what we built our peace on," she said, her voice amplified by the plaza's strange acoustics. "A lie that became true because we needed it to be. But we don't need it anymore." She looked at the faces below, the faces in the balconies, the faces of the Sentinels who had lowered their weapons. "We have something better. We have the memory of what it cost."

A delegate from the southern provinces stood, an older man with a face like weathered stone. "What do we do with it?"

Kaela thought of Cassian, of Alric, of the baker's boy who had died for a story. "We carry it," she said. "We carry it together."

She turned to leave, but a Sentinel blocked her path—not to arrest her, but to kneel. He placed his rifle on the ground. Others followed, a ripple of metal and submission that spread through the plaza.

Elara watched them, her face unreadable. "They'll call you a hero."

"I'll be gone before they can," Kaela said. "Heroes are just people who become symbols. And I've had enough of symbols."

But Mira was looking at the kneeling Sentinels, at the delegates who were helping each other stand, at the way the glass walls of the plaza seemed to let in more light than before. "Maybe symbols can change," she said.

Kaela followed her gaze. The dagger on the lectern was still glowing, its light soft and steady. Not a weapon's glow, but a lantern's.

They left the plaza quietly, through a servant's passage that Elara still remembered. Behind them, the city was changing. It would be chaos for a while. There would be more blood, more betrayal, more attempts to shape the story to suit one side or another.

But the memory was out. Like smoke, like disease, like love—it would spread.

## Part V: The Weight of Air

Two weeks later, Kaela stood on the roof of her studio, watching the twin suns of Vespera set. The city below was quieter than it had been. The Ministry still existed, but it was fractured, its authority questioned by its own Sentinels. The peace summit had become something else—a negotiation between equals, messy and slow. The Chimera had declared a temporary ceasefire, though Selene had sent word that it was fragile as eggshell.

Mira was inside, teaching herself to read from a book Elara had sent—one of the secret histories she had kept in her mind. The girl had decided she wanted to be a chronicler, not a forger. She wanted to write things down, permanent and unchangeable.

There had been no notes from the landlord. The building had been purchased by an anonymous benefactor—Elara, Kaela suspected—who had declared rent suspended indefinitely. The whole Lower Ward was like that now, in a state of suspended uncertainty. People were waiting to see what kind of city would emerge from the memory of what had been.

The door behind her opened. Elara stepped onto the roof, no longer in servant's livery but in simple wool and linen. She looked smaller without the Ministry's trappings, more human.

"I brought you something," she said, holding out a small package

wrapped in brown paper. Kaela took it, feeling the weight of something solid and round. She unwrapped it slowly, the paper falling away like shed skin.

Inside was a pocket watch. Gold plating, worn thin. Its glass face was cracked in a spiderweb pattern. Inside, time had stopped at precisely 11:47.

"The client died," Elara said softly. "Three days after the Summit. His heart gave out. He was old, and the shock of the truth..." She paused. "He left instructions. Said you had done what he asked—made it remember peace. He wanted you to have it."

Kaela turned the watch over in her hands. The memory inside it was faint now, almost gone, but she could still feel the echo of what she had forged: the quiet after the Great Strike, the twin suns setting, Mira's hand in hers. It felt like a lifetime ago.

"He understood," Kaela said. "In the end."

"They're calling it the Remembering," Elara said, looking out over the city. "People are sharing memories in the streets. Not just the big histories, but small ones. What their grandmother's kitchen smelled like. The way their child laughed before the war. They're realizing that memory isn't something that can be forged by one hand. It's something we build together."

"How very messy," Kaela said, but there was no bitterness in it.

"Yes." Elara smiled. "It's terrible. It's chaotic. It's the most beautiful thing I've ever seen."

They stood in silence for a while, watching the suns dip below the Spire. The city lights began to flicker on—not the harsh gaslight of the Ministry's design, but softer, warmer lights that people had begun to install in their homes, their shops, their own small acts of rebellion against the perpetual twilight.

"Selene's been arrested," Elara said quietly. "The Chimera leadership. They were going to launch another bombing campaign. She tried to stop them, but..." She trailed off. "She sent a message. Said to tell you that some revolutions eat their own, but the Remembering doesn't have to."

Kaela thought of the woman in the brass mask, the way her voice had cracked when she talked about truth. "She'll be executed."

"Probably. But she'll be remembered. Accurately." Elara turned to her. "That's the trade we're making now. Truth for consequences. No more erasures."

"What about the Archives?"

"Open to the public. Scholars from the Outer Provinces are coming to study them. They're... horrified. But they're also fascinated. They've never had access to their own history before." Elara's voice held a note of wonder. "It's like watching a city learn to read."

Mira appeared in the doorway, the book clutched to her chest. "Kaela? There's someone at the door. A Sentinel."

Kaela's spine straightened, old instincts flaring. But Mira shook her head. "He doesn't have a rifle. He says he's here to learn."

"To learn what?" Kaela asked.

"How to forget." Mira's smile was small and sharp. "He says he has memories from the war he can't live with. He heard you could help."

Kaela looked at the watch in her hand, at the cracked glass that had sealed itself with acceptance. She thought of all the memories she had taken, all the truths she had buried, all the lies she had told herself about being small.

"Tell him I don't do that anymore," she said. "Tell him I don't take memories away. But I can help him carry them."

Mira nodded, disappearing back inside. Elara watched her go. "She's grown."

"She was always grown," Kaela said. "The city just didn't see it."

"What will you do now?"

Kaela considered the question. The Ministry was fractured. The Chimera was splintering. The city was learning to tell its own stories. There would always be a need for forgers—people who could shape memory, who could help others carry the weight. But she had a different idea.
