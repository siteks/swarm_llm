# The Paper Garden of Shanghai

## Prologue: The Dream

The dead came to Li Mei on paper wings. In her dreams, they fluttered through latticed windows, their bodies translucent as rice paper, rustling with the sound of old letters. They came with the summer monsoons, when the Huangpu River swelled and the foreign ships on the Bund sounded their horns like lost beasts. They came with their memories still burning inside them, and they asked her to remember.

She always woke before she could.

On the morning of her twenty-first birthday, in the fourth year of the Republic, Mei woke to the smell of ink and mourning. Her father, Li Han, was burning offerings in the courtyard again—paper houses, paper servants, paper money for the ancestors. The smoke curled through the shikumen house like a ghost's breath, carrying with it the scent of camphor and grief.

"Another dream?" her mother asked, not looking up from the breakfast she was preparing. Mother was practical in that way. Dreams were like the foreigners in the Concessions—unwelcome, but you learned to step around them.

Mei nodded, pulling her black hair into a tight braid. "The woman with the jade comb. She kept trying to give it to me."

Mother's knife paused against the radish she was slicing. "Your grandmother wore a jade comb. She died before the fall of the Qing. You never knew her."

"I know the comb."

Downstairs, Father's cough echoed through the house—a wet, tearing sound that had been getting worse since winter. He was dying. Everyone knew it but him, or perhaps he knew and simply folded the knowledge away, like a precious paper pattern kept from the damp.

The Li family had made paper effigies for the dead for three hundred years. In the old days, before the foreigners carved Shanghai into pieces with their treaties and their gunboats, before the Republic and the warlords and the endless revolutions, the Lis had served emperors. Their paper funeral offerings were legend—minghun brides for unmarried sons, paper palaces for important officials, entire shadow armies for generals who died with unfinished business.

Now they made do with merchants and shopkeepers. And Father was the last master of their craft.

Mei was his apprentice. His daughter. His disappointment.

She finished dressing—cotton tunic, trousers, the plain practical clothes of a working girl—and went down to the workshop. The house was narrow and deep, like most shikumen in the Chinese City, built around a shared courtyard where neighbors washed clothes and complained about the foreigners and the latest taxes. Their workshop fronted the street, with living quarters behind. It was dark, always dark, to protect the paper from the humidity that could rot it before the paint dried.

Father sat at his workbench, a single lamp illuminating the half-formed figure before him. It was a man, life-sized, with limbs of bamboo frame and skin of rice paper. The face had been painted with delicate strokes—eyes like wet ink, lips curved in the suggestion of a smile. A wedding effigy, for a woman who'd died young and needed a husband in the afterlife.

"Qingming Festival approaches," Father said without turning. "The Zhang family commissioned it. Their daughter died of influenza last month. Twenty years old."

The same age as Mei. She watched the paper man, his blank eyes seeming to look through her. Did the dead care if their paper spouses were beautiful? Did they notice the craft?

"Tell me about the dream," Father said. He always knew.

Mei described the woman—the green silk dress, the jade comb, the way she kept pressing it into Mei's hand, saying, *"He must remember. He must."*

Father was quiet for a long time. Then he reached into his robe and pulled out a small paper figure, no larger than his palm. A woman, delicately painted, wearing green.

"This was my mother's work," he said. "Keep it with you. Dreams are just memories that haven't found the right paper yet."

He pressed it into her hand. His fingers were cold. When Mei looked down at the figure, she saw it wore a jade comb in its paper hair.

That night, the foreign ships fired their guns in celebration. Some treaty signed, some distant victory in Europe. The sound rattled the windows and shook dust from the beams. In the International Settlement, the foreigners danced and drank champagne. In the Chinese City, people pulled their children close and waited for the next calamity.

Mei held the paper grandmother in her palm and listened to her father cough until dawn.

He was gone by morning.

## Chapter 1: The Apprentice

They buried Father in the old way, though Mother complained about the cost. A paper palace burned at his graveside, along with paper servants, a paper horse, and a paper rickshaw pulled by a paper boy. The smoke was black and thick and smelled of money. Mei had made most of them herself—her first major commission, paid for in tears and ash.

After the funeral, Uncle Zhen appeared. He was Father's younger brother, though he'd left the family trade years ago to work in the French Concession as a clerk for a British trading company. He wore a Western suit that didn't quite fit, and his shoes were polished to a high shine that reflected nothing of the dust of the Chinese City.

"The workshop must close," he announced, standing in the darkened front room as if the very shadows offended him. "The rent is too high, the trade is dying. Who needs paper effigies when there are photographers? When the young people want to be modern?"

Mei stood beside Mother, who had become somehow smaller in the week since Father's death. "I am the workshop now," Mei said. "I am Father's apprentice."

Uncle Zhen laughed, a short bark of sound. "You are a girl. The customers will not accept a woman's work for their ancestors. It is unlucky."

"The customers accepted Father's work," Mei said. "And Father taught me everything."

"He taught you what he could. But you are not a son."

The words landed like a slap. They were true, of course. In all of Shanghai's twisting alleys and grand boulevards, in the shadow of the foreign banks and the Chinese guilds, truth was the one commodity still traded freely. Mei was not a son. She was merely herself, and herself was not enough.

Uncle Zhen left them with a final warning: three months to vacate, or he would sell the shikumen to a developer who wanted to build a modern apartment block for Chinese merchants eager to imitate their foreign masters.

That night, Mei went to the workshop and lit the lamp. She pulled out Father's ledgers—accounts going back decades, written in his elegant hand. Among them, she found a small notebook she didn't recognize. Its leather cover was soft from handling, its pages filled with sketches of paper figures unlike any she'd seen. Not effigies for the dead, but something else—paper birds with strange characters on their wings, paper boats that seemed to fold into infinity, paper hearts that beat with ink.

On the last page, in Father's shaking script: *The memory papers. For when forgetting is a greater death than dying.*

Mei turned the page. There was an address, written in English: *18 Rue du Consulat, French Concession.*

She knew the street. It was where the rich foreigners lived, in houses with gardens and servants and gates that kept out the smell of the Chinese City. Why would Father have an address there?

The paper grandmother in her pocket felt warm. When Mei took her out, she saw that the figure had changed. Its painted eyes now looked directly at her, and in its tiny hand it held something that hadn't been there before—a microscopic paper scroll.

Mei had to use Father's magnifying glass to read it.

*He must remember. The garden is dying. Come.*

It was signed with a name she didn't recognize: *Sofia Chen.*

## Chapter 2: The Foreign Devil

The French Concession was a world removed. Crossing from the Chinese City into its tree-lined streets was like stepping through a paper screen into another dimension. The smells changed—less fish and incense, more perfume and motor oil. The sounds changed—rickshaw bells gave way to automobile horns. The very air felt different, lighter somehow, as if it had been filtered through money.

Mei had been here only once before, as a child, when Father had delivered a commission to a French missionary who wanted paper angels for his church. She remembered being struck by how clean everything was, how the foreigners moved through the streets as if they owned them. Which, of course, they did.

Number 18 Rue du Consulat was a villa behind a high iron gate, its walls covered in creeping vines that choked the stone. It looked abandoned, or perhaps just forgotten, a piece of old France left to decay in the Chinese sun. Mei pushed the gate. It opened with a scream of rust.

The garden was a jungle. What had once been manicured hedges and flower beds had grown wild, a tangle of roses and jasmine and something that might have been wisteria. In the center stood a dead tree, its branches like gnarled fingers reaching for a sky it could no longer touch.

A woman sat beneath it, on a stone bench so covered in moss it seemed to have grown from the earth itself. She was Chinese, but dressed in foreign clothes—a silk dress of deep blue, cut in the Western style, with pearls at her throat. She was old, perhaps sixty, but her beauty had not so much faded as refined itself into something sharp and clear as crystal.

"You must be Li Han's daughter," she said. Her voice held the trace of a French accent, a lilting quality that made each word sound like a question. "You look like him around the eyes."

"Who are you?" Mei asked.

"Sofia Chen. Once, I was Li Chen Sofie. Your father and I were... friends. Before he married your mother."

Mei felt the ground shift. Father had never spoken of anyone in the Concessions. His life had been the workshop, the ancestors, the paper dead.

"He sent you a message," Sofia said. "Before he died. Did you receive it?"

"The paper figure. It... changed."

Sofia smiled. "Yes. That sounds like his work. He was the only one who could make them truly live."

"Memory papers," Mei said. "That's what he called them."

"Come inside. The garden is not safe for such conversations."

The villa's interior was a museum of decay. Silk wallpaper peeled from the walls like old skin. Furniture draped in white sheets stood like sleeping giants. But in a room at the back, behind a locked door that Sofia opened with a silver key, there was a library that seemed untouched by time.

The shelves were lined with paper. Not books—paper figures, thousands of them, each different. Birds, animals, people, abstract shapes that seemed to shift if you looked at them too long. They were perfectly preserved, sealed behind glass.

"My collection," Sofia said. "Your father's life's work. The true work, not the funeral offerings. These are memory keepers."

She took one down—a small paper boat, delicate as a dragonfly's wing. "This holds the memory of my mother's hands. When I hold it, I can feel her making dumplings, the dough sticky against my fingers. This—" she indicated a paper bird "—holds my first sight of the sea. Your father could take a memory and fold it into paper. Not a copy. The thing itself."

Mei thought of her dreams. The woman with the jade comb. "Why?"

"Because some memories are too dangerous to keep in the mind. Because sometimes you need to forget, but you cannot afford to lose. Because the foreigners are not the only ones who know how to steal." Sofia's face hardened. "Your father helped me hide something. Something the British want very badly. Now that he's gone, they will come for me. And for you."

"Why me? I know nothing."

"You are his heir. You have his hands." Sofia took Mei's hands in hers, turning them over. "Yes. The same long fingers, the same calluses from the bamboo knife. You can learn to read the memories, to make them yourself. You can finish what he started."

From a drawer, she pulled a larger paper figure—a man, dressed in the uniform of a British soldier, but with Chinese eyes. "This is what they want. What your father died protecting."

The paper soldier's eyes were unfinished. Blank ovals waiting for pupils, for life.

"He was not a soldier," Sofia said softly. "He was my brother. He died in the Boxer Rebellion, but not as the British claim. He died because he knew the truth about who funded the rebellion, who armed the foreign troops, who profited from the blood. He was going to testify before a British inquiry. He died first."

Mei looked at the unfinished eyes. "Father was making a memory paper of him?"

"Your father was going to give him back his testimony. A dead man cannot be silenced. A paper man can speak forever." Sofia's voice broke. "But he ran out of time."

Outside, a car door slammed. Sofia moved to the window, her composure returning like a mask. "They are here. The man in the car is Julian Hawthorne, a British diplomat who collects Chinese art. He collects other things too. Secrets. Lives."

Mei's heart hammered. "What do I do?"

"You run. Take this." Sofia pressed the paper soldier into Mei's hand. It felt warm, alive. "Finish it. Bring him to the Garden of the Purple Bamboo by the West Lake in Hangzhou. I will meet you there in one month. If I am not there, give it to the old monk who tends the temple. He will know what to do."

"How do I finish it? I don't know how!"

"Your father taught you. You just don't remember yet."

Footsteps on the gravel drive. Sofia pushed Mei toward a back door. "Go through the garden. There is a gap in the wall. Run!"

Mei ran. Behind her, she heard voices—Sofia's, cultured and calm, and a man's, smooth as silk but edged with steel. She didn't look back. She clutched the paper soldier to her chest and fled through the overgrown garden, thorns tearing at her clothes.

The gap in the wall was there, just as Sofia promised. Mei squeezed through, finding herself in an alley behind the villa. She ran until her lungs burned, until the foreign streets gave way to Chinese streets, until the paper soldier in her hands began to grow warm, then hot.

She stopped in a small temple courtyard, hidden from the street

The paper soldier in her hands began to burn—not with fire, but with memory. Images flooded Mei's mind: a young man laughing beneath a willow tree, the smell of gunpowder mixing with cherry blossoms, a voice shouting in English, *"They'll never know the difference!"* The sensations were so vivid she stumbled, catching herself against the temple wall. The stone was cool against her palm, but inside her head, she was somewhere else, sometime else.

She looked down. The soldier's blank eyes now held the faintest shadows of pupils, ink bleeding into rice paper as if drawn by an invisible brush.

"You shouldn't hold that so openly," a voice said.

Mei spun. An old woman sat in the temple's doorway, smoking a long bamboo pipe. She wore the dress of a street vendor, but her eyes held the sharpness of a scholar. "They can feel it, you know. The ones who hunt such things. Like a fire in a dark room."

"Who are you?"

"Someone who knew your father when he was young and foolish." The woman tapped her pipe against the stone. "Before he became old and foolish. I am called Auntie Ying, though I am nobody's aunt."

The paper soldier pulsed warm against Mei's chest. She realized she had tucked it inside her tunic without thinking. "Did he send you?"

"He sent you. That is enough." Auntie Ying stood with surprising ease. "Come. The British devil's men are already searching the French Concession. They have dogs that smell paper and ink."

She led Mei through a maze of alleys that seemed to fold in on themselves like poorly made origami. They passed through streets Mei had known her whole life, yet they looked different now—dangerous, watchful. Every rickshaw that passed might contain a spy. Every foreign face might hide Julian Hawthorne's eyes.

They ended up in a part of the Chinese City that Mei barely knew, near the old city walls that the foreigners kept promising to tear down. Here, the houses leaned together like conspirators, their roofs almost touching overhead, creating a tunnel of shadow. Auntie Ying stopped at a door that looked like every other door and produced a key.

Inside was a paper workshop—but not like her father's. This one was alive. Paper cranes hung from the ceiling, slowly flapping their wings as if moved by a breeze only they could feel. Paper tigers prowled along the shelves, their flat bodies rippling with painted stripes. And everywhere, the sound of rustling, like dry leaves whispering secrets.

"My own small rebellion," Auntie Ying said, seeing Mei's expression. "While your father made memories for the dead, I make them for the living. A different art, but the same root."

She led Mei to a workbench and gestured for her to take out the soldier. The paper figure seemed heavier now, denser with the weight of borrowed life.

"Your father taught you the basics," Auntie Ying said. It wasn't a question. "The bamboo frame, the rice paper skin, the ink from pine soot and bone glue. But he didn't teach you the breath."

"The breath?"

"The moment you put yourself into the paper. When your memory becomes its memory." She held up her own hands, gnarled and stained with ink. "Your father was the master of preserving what was lost. But you—" she peered at Mei with eyes like black needles "—you have the gift for waking what was never allowed to live."

Auntie Ying placed a bamboo brush in Mei's hand. The handle was worn smooth by decades of use. "Finish his eyes. But be careful. When you give him sight, he will see. And what he sees, he will remember."

Mei dipped the brush in ink that seemed to move of its own accord, thick and dark as liquid night. She held her breath. Her father had taught her to paint eyes last, because they were the doorway to the soul, and once opened, could not be closed again. But he had never taught her to paint eyes that held a dead man's testament.

She touched brush to paper.

The memory hit her like a physical blow. She was in a room—a foreign room, with heavy curtains and the smell of leather and tobacco. A British officer sat across from her, though she was seeing through someone else's eyes. The officer's face was blurred, like a watercolor left in the rain, but his voice was clear: *"The Boxers were useful fools. Their rebellion let us consolidate power, seize more concessions. Your sister Sofia understands this. She profits from it. Why can't you?"*

The memory-voice that was not hers responded: *"Because I have seen the accounting books. I know who really sold the guns to the Boxers. I know whose banks held the indemnities. And I will testify."*

The officer leaned forward. *"Then you are a dead man, Chen. You just don't know it yet."*

The memory shattered. Mei found herself on the floor, the brush clutched in her hand, ink spilled across the boards. The paper soldier lay on the workbench, his eyes now complete—deep brown, intelligent, filled with a sorrow that transcended paper and ink.

"There," Auntie Ying said softly. "Now he can tell his story. But not yet. First, you must learn to control what you wake."

She helped Mei to her feet. "Your father made a mistake. He tried to hide the soldier in the city, where the British have eyes everywhere. You must take it out of Shanghai. But not to Hangzhou. That is the first place they'll look."

"But Sofia said—"

"Sofia is brave, but she is not a paper artist. She does not understand how these things are hunted." Auntie Ying moved to a cabinet and pulled out a flat package wrapped in silk. "Your father prepared for this. He knew he was dying—his lungs had turned to rot from the camphor and the ink. He made his own memory, for you."

She unwrapped the silk. Inside was a paper map—not of Shanghai, but of somewhere else. As Mei watched, the map began to fold itself, mountains rising, rivers running, towns appearing like ink blooming in water. It was a map of memory, a map that could hide itself.

"Where is this?" Mei asked.

"The place where memories go to be forgotten. The Garden of the Purple Bamboo is real, but it is not in Hangzhou. It is where Hangzhou used to be, before the foreigners renamed everything." Auntie Ying traced a finger along a river that hadn't existed in fifty years. "Your father learned the old folding, the techniques from before the foreigners came. He learned how to make paper that remembers places, not just people. This map will take you to the real garden. But you must follow it exactly, and you must not let anyone else see it."

The paper map fluttered into Mei's hand, light as a moth. She could feel the weight of her father's memory in it—the precise way he had held the bamboo knife, the particular mixture of ink, the chant he had murmured under his breath while folding.

"One more thing," Auntie Ying said. She pulled out a small paper bird, no larger than a sparrow. "This will go with you. It carries a memory of your father, the sound of his voice. When you are lost, it will sing."

Mei took the bird. Its wings were made of old letters, the characters still visible. She recognized her father's handwriting.

A commotion outside made them both freeze. Dogs barking. Men's voices speaking English.

"Too late," Auntie Ying whispered. "They've found us."

She grabbed Mei's arm and pulled her toward the back of the workshop, where a curtain of hanging paper cranes concealed a door. "Go through. It leads to the sewers. Follow the rats—they know the way out. When you emerge, you will be beyond the city walls. From there, the map will guide you."

"What about you?"

"I am an old woman who makes paper toys. They will question me, find nothing, and leave." She pressed something into Mei's hand—a small paper knife, its blade impossibly sharp. "Your father's tool. He wanted you to have it. Now go!"

The door opened onto darkness and the stench of forgotten water. Behind her, Mei heard the front door splinter. She clutched the soldier, the map, the bird, and the knife to her chest, and stepped into the dark.

The sewers were a paper world inverted—instead of clean sheets and sharp folds, there was muck and rot. But as Mei's eyes adjusted, she saw that someone had been here before. The walls were marked with paper lanterns, each one lighting as she approached, guiding her path. They were her father's work—she recognized the brushstrokes, the way the light danced inside the paper like captured fireflies.

She followed them for what felt like hours, the sound of pursuit fading behind her. The paper bird in her pocket began to sing—not with sound, but with memory. She heard her father's voice as clearly as if he walked beside her:

*"Mei, if you are hearing this, I am gone. Do not mourn me. Death is just another kind of folding. What matters is that you remember: the British did not come to China for tea or for trade. They came for our memories. They try to rewrite what we remember, to make us believe we were always weak, always backward. Your gift is to preserve the truth. The soldier carries Sofia's brother's testimony, yes, but more than that—he carries proof that the British armed the Boxers to justify their own invasion. Without that proof, they will claim the moral high ground forever."*

The voice paused, and Mei could almost feel her father's hand on her shoulder.

*"But there is more. The soldier also carries a memory of the indemnities—the real accounting of what the foreign banks seized from China after the rebellion. If that becomes public, it could topple governments. That is why they hunt it. That is why they will hunt you."*

The lanterns ahead flickered, and Mei saw daylight filtering through a grate. She emerged into a field of wild grass, the city walls behind her, the river ahead. She was beyond the foreign concessions, beyond the Chinese City, in the liminal space where Shanghai became China again.

The paper map fluttered in her hands, its folds shifting. A new path appeared, leading west. Not toward Hangzhou, but toward the mountains, toward a place that existed only in memory.

She took her first step, and the soldier in her satchel seemed to sigh. The paper bird in her pocket sang its silent song. Behind her, Shanghai burned with its usual fires—factory smoke, cooking fires, the invisible flames of a thousand paper memories waiting to be lit.

She did not look back.

## Chapter 3: The River of Paper

Three days on the road taught Mei that the world was larger than Shanghai, but no less dangerous. The river towns were full of soldiers—warlord troops, Republican militia, foreign gunboats that prowled the waterways like iron dragons. Everyone was looking for something: deserters, smugglers, revolutionaries. Mei learned to keep her satchel close and her eyes down.

She traveled as a paper seller, the most obvious disguise. In her basket she carried funeral offerings—simple paper houses, generic paper servants, the kind of mass-produced effigies that families burned for their ancestors when they couldn't afford a master craftsman. She was just another merchant's daughter, scrabbling for a living in a country that seemed to be tearing itself apart.

But inside her basket, hidden beneath the cheap offerings, were the soldier, the map, the bird, and her father's knife. They were warm against her back, a constant reminder that she was no longer just a paper artist. She was a smuggler of memories.

On the fourth day, she reached Suzhou, the city of gardens and silk. The map had led her here, though she didn't know why. She found an inn by the canals, a place so old the wood seemed to be turning back into paper. The proprietor, a woman with no teeth and sharp eyes, took one look at Mei's satchel and said, "You're the Li girl."

Mei froze, her hand inching toward the paper knife.

"The paper gives you away," the old woman said, nodding at the satchel. "It smells of Li Han's ink. I am called Old Mrs. Wang. Your father saved my husband's memory after the Taiping Rebellion. He is dead now, but I have his voice in a paper cricket. When I am lonely, I let it chirp." She smiled, showing pink gums. "You have his eyes, too. The same look of someone who sees too much."

"I need to stay one night," Mei said. "Then I go west."

"West?" Mrs. Wang's voice dropped. "The mountains are full of bandits. And worse—foreign missionaries who take children and turn them into little Englishmen. But you already know about foreigners, don't you?"

She led Mei to a room overlooking the canal. As night fell, Mei spread the map on the floor. It had changed again. Now it showed Suzhou, but overlaid with another city—a ghost city of canals and bridges that no longer existed. A path led through this phantom city to a garden that was both there and not there.

The paper bird sang: *"Suzhou holds the key to the soldier's voice. Your father left something here, with Mrs. Wang. Ask her about the peony."*

Mei went downstairs. The old woman was boiling tea over a small brazier.

"My father left something with you," Mei said. "A peony."

Mrs. Wang's face closed like a paper fan. "Peonies are for the living. They mean prosperity, good fortune. Your father would not leave such a thing."

But her eyes flickered toward a cabinet in the corner. Mei moved toward it.

"Do not!" Mrs. Wang cried, but it was too late. Mei pulled open the door.

Inside, a paper peony bloomed. It was perfect, each petal so lifelike she expected to smell its perfume. But this was no ordinary flower—painted on its petals, in characters so small they were almost invisible, was a single name: *Hawthorne.*

And beneath it, dates, places, sums of money. A ledger of corruption written in ink that shimmered like fresh blood.

"The British paymaster," Mrs. Wang whispered. "Your father stole it from the concession offices. That is why they hunt you. Not just for the soldier, but for the accounting. With that, you can prove that the same British banks that funded the Boxer indemnities also funded the rebellion itself. They created the crisis they claimed to solve."

The peony was trembling. Mei realized it was singing, a high sweet sound of paper vibrating. It was afraid.

From the canal outside, she heard the sound of a motorboat. Not the puttering engines of Chinese fishermen, but the smooth purr of a foreign craft. Voices speaking English.

Mrs. Wang moved faster than seemed possible for her age. She snatched the peony from Mei's hands and pressed a different paper flower into them—a chrysanthemum, the flower of death. "Burn this when you are safe. It will hide your trail. Now, out the back!"

Mei ran through a door that opened onto a narrow canal. A small boat waited, poled by a boy who couldn't have been more than twelve. He gave her a gap-toothed grin.

"Father said you'd come," the boy said. "He said the paper would sing when it was time."

"Who is your father?"

"Auntie Ying's nephew. We are the paper runners. We keep the memories moving."

He poled them into the darkness of the canal, beneath bridges so low they had to duck. Behind them, Mei heard shouts, the crack of splintering wood. She smelled smoke—Mrs. Wang burning something. The chrysanthemum, perhaps, and with it, the trail.

The boy took her to the edge of the city, where the canals met the river. "Follow the river west," he said. "The map will show you when to leave the water. And here—" he pressed a folded paper into her hand "—a gift. It's not a memory, just a promise."

She unfolded it. A paper fish, its scales made of tiny characters that read: *We remember. We resist.*

"Thank you," Mei whispered.

The boy smiled. "

The boy's promise warmed her palm as the boat slipped into the current. The paper fish seemed to swim against her skin, its characters pulsing with each beat of her heart. Behind her, Suzhou's canals burned—torchlight reflecting off water, the shouts of men who thought they could capture shadows in a net. Mrs. Wang's smoke rose black against the moon, carrying away the scent of ink and betrayal.

The Yangtze was a different beast than the canals. It moved with the ponderous inevitability of history, carrying junks and steamers, fishing boats and gunboats, all swept along in its silt-heavy flow. Mei huddled in the stern of a cargo junk, her satchel pressed between her knees, the map a glowing warmth against her chest. The captain, a taciturn man with one eye and no questions, had accepted the paper bird Auntie Ying had given her as payment. When he held it, his face had softened, and Mei knew it held some memory he couldn't bear to lose—a daughter, perhaps, or a wife stolen by the warlord wars.

"West," he had grunted. "But no further than Wuhu. The river belongs to General Zhang after that, and he takes a dim view of paper artists."

"Why?"

The captain had laughed, a sound like grinding stone. "Because paper artists remember what he'd rather forget. And because the British pay him well to deliver any strange girls with stranger cargo."

Now, three days on the water, Mei watched the shore slide past—endless fields of rice and sorghum, villages that huddled against the water like frightened children, the occasional foreign concession with its electric lights blazing defiance against the night. She learned to read the river's language: the way the current spoke of floods and famine, how the smell of the water changed when they passed a city, how the birds avoided certain stretches where the dead lay thick beneath the silt.

The soldier in her satchel grew heavier each day. Not with paper, but with expectation. Sometimes, when the moon was high and the watchman dozed, she would take him out and study his face. The eyes her brush had given him seemed to follow her, and sometimes she caught a whisper of thought—not her own—*Will she be brave enough? Will I speak in time?*

The fourth night, a fog rolled in so thick it turned the world into a blank page. The junk dropped anchor, and the crew huddled in their bunks, muttering about river ghosts and foreign devils who used the mist to move unseen. Mei stayed on deck, the map spread across her knees. In the fog, its lines glowed faint phosphorescent green, showing not just the river but the ghost rivers beneath it—ancient courses, forgotten tributaries, the paths of water that had flowed before the foreigners came.

A small light appeared on the water, bobbing like a will-o'-wisp. The paper bird in her pocket began to sing its silent song: *Danger. Memory. Truth.*

The light resolved into a lantern hanging from a small sampan, poled by a figure in a conical hat. As it drew alongside, Mei saw it was a woman, her face hidden in shadow.

"You carry the peony," the woman said. Her voice carried across the water without effort, as if the fog itself were speaking.

"I don't have it," Mei said. "It was left behind."

"You carry its memory. That is enough." The woman poled her sampan closer. "The British have offered ten thousand taels for a paper artist traveling west. General Zhang's men are at every dock. They have a dog that smells truth. It will find you."

Mei's hand crept toward the knife.

The woman laughed. "I am not here to collect. I am here to fold." She held up her own hands—missing two fingers on the left, the sign of a paper artist who had lost a duel with her own creation. "I am called the River Wife. I make paper that forgets."

She tossed something onto the deck. It landed with a soft thump—a paper dog, exquisitely folded, its ears pricked forward.

"No," Auntie Ying warned. "He is a master—"

"I accept," Mei said. She had to. The longer he stood there, the more the room seemed to be folding around him, the walls bending toward his will.

The duel took place in the space between heartbeats. Ashworth moved first, his fingers creating a paper dragon that breathed smoke made of forgotten treaties. Mei countered with a crane, her father's bird, which caught the smoke and turned it into cherry blossoms.

Back and forth they folded. He made a tank that crushed the blossoms; she made a kite that lifted the tank into the sky. He folded a church that whispered prayers of submission; she folded a temple bell that rang with resistance. Each creation lived for only a moment before being undone by the next, until the air between them was thick with paper scraps that held half-formed ideas.

Ashworth was faster, more experienced. But Mei had something he didn't: the weight of being real. He folded from theory, from British schools of abstract magic. She folded from bone and blood, from watching her father die, from the river that now carried Chen Tian's testimony to the sea.

She made a mistake. Deliberately. She folded a soldier that was weak in the knees, clumsy in the hands. Ashworth seized it, folding it into his own creation—a British general who would command her army.

But as his fingers worked, the soldier dissolved into characters. The truth Mei had written spilled out, covering his hands. The dates, the names, the proof of British treachery—all of it crawled up his arms like black vines.

"This is not folding," he gasped. "This is... this is *infection*."

"Yes," Mei said. "Truth is contagious."

Ashworth tried to cut the characters away with his knife, but each slice only multiplied them. The truth had found a host in the man who thought he controlled it. He began to speak in a voice not his own, reciting the dates of shipments, the names of conspirators, the amount of silver paid to turn Chinese against Chinese.

His tiger and dove collapsed. The paper army stood untouched.

Auntie Ying moved forward, holding a simple sheet of rice paper. "This is your passage home," she said to Ashworth. "A one-way ticket to a London that will not remember you."

She folded him into it. It took twelve of the Folded Ones to hold him down while she worked, his scream becoming the sound of rustling paper, then silence. When they were done, a single paper figure lay on the floor—a man in a black suit, his face blank, his hands frozen in a folding gesture. Mei recognized the style. It was how Ashworth had disposed of his wife.

They burned him in the pulping vats, his ash mixing with the ink that would make new paper. Let him be part of the medium he tried to control.

Sofia went west, carrying ten of the soldiers. She would plant them in the mountains where the warlords ruled, in villages that had never seen a foreigner. She would tell them her brother's story, and her own.

Auntie Ying stayed with the Folded Ones, preparing for the next battle. "There will always be men like Ashworth," she said. "But now there will always be a Mei to fold against them."

As for Mei, she returned to Shanghai. Not as a fugitive, but as a merchant's daughter with a basket of funeral offerings. The city was different now. The British still ruled the concessions, the warlords still fought, but in the alleys and courtyards, paper soldiers had begun to appear. They hung in teashops, tucked into the beams of houses, floating in bowls of water set out for ancestors.

People found them and read them, and they remembered.

In the French Concession, Julian Hawthorne received a package. Inside was a paper effigy of himself, and when he unfolded it, he found his own crimes written on its skin. He left Shanghai the next week, recalled to London in disgrace.

On the Bund, a British gunboat sank suddenly, without explanation. The crew reported that the water had become heavy with memories, too dense to sail through.

And in a small shikumen house that had somehow escaped development, Li Mei's mother waited. When Mei walked through the door, her mother looked at her stained hands and said, "You have been folding."

"Yes," Mei said.

"Good," her mother replied. "The ancestors were getting lonely."

She opened a cabinet. Inside, hundreds of paper figures waited—new ancestors, new memories, a whole paper world that had grown in Mei's absence. And at the front, a small figure of a man with kind eyes and ink-stained fingers.

Mei took it. It was warm, and it hummed with a familiar voice: *"Welcome home, daughter. The work begins again."*

She set it on the workbench, next to her father's tools. Outside, Shanghai burned with its usual fires, but now there were also lanterns—paper lanterns, drifting on every breeze, each one a memory that could not be extinguished.

She picked up the bamboo knife and began to fold. Not soldiers this time, but gardens. Gardens that would grow in every heart that remembered. Gardens where paper and flesh could coexist, where the dead could speak, and where the living could finally learn to listen.

The paper ship sailed on, invisible now, carrying its cargo of truth to every corner of a China that was learning to remember itself.

The paper soldiers worked better than they could have imagined—and that was the problem.

Within a month of Mei's return to Shanghai, the British Concession was in crisis. Hawthorne's replacement, a pinch-faced man named Cartwright, found his desk covered in paper effigies each morning—tiny soldiers that whispered bank account numbers when he tried to sleep. The French reported similar occurrences: paper boats clogging their harbor, each one inscribed with details of missionary gun-running. In the Chinese City, warlord troops confiscated hundreds of the figures, only to have their own men start reciting the testimony, word for word, in their sleep.

The truth, once folded, could not be contained. It had a life of its own.

Mei heard these reports from her workshop window, where she now made only neutral things—paper flowers for festivals, generic ancestors for families who couldn't afford better. The real work happened at night, in the hidden room beneath the floorboards where her father had once stored his tools. There, she crafted the next generation of memory keepers: not soldiers, but seeds. Paper dandelions that would drift on the wind, carrying whispers of rebellion. Paper rice grains that would hide in sacks, passing through countless hands before releasing their stories.

Her mother, once practical, now helped with the folding. The White Lotus women had taught her their own paper arts—how to make lotus blossoms that could hide a woman from soldiers' eyes, how to fold prayers so small they could be swallowed if searched. She brought these techniques home like contraband wisdom.

"You are making them desperate," Mother warned one evening, watching Mei fold a particularly delicate rice grain. "Desperate men do desperate things."

She was right.

The retaliation came on the fifth of the month, during the Qingming Festival when families burned offerings for their dead. This year, the smoke over Shanghai smelled wrong—acrid, chemical. People who inhaled it fell sick, their memories fracturing. They forgot their children's names, their own addresses, the faces of their ancestors. The British claimed it was a new strain of influenza, but the paper artists knew better.

They were burning *counter-memories*.

Cartwright had brought in a new expert—a woman this time, which surprised Mei. Miss Peregrine Whitmore looked like a governess from a Dickens novel: stern bun, sharper eyes, fingers that moved with the clinical precision of a surgeon. She didn't fold paper. She *unfolded* it, strip by strip, until nothing remained but pulp and regret.

"They call her the Librarian," Auntie Ying's message said, delivered by a paper moth that dissolved after speaking. "She works for the British Museum, cataloguing colonized memories. She has a furnace that burns at the temperature of forgetting."

Whitmore's method was surgical and precise. She targeted the paper soldiers one by one, tracing them through the supply chains and confiscating them. But she didn't stop there. She began collecting *all* paper art—genuine funeral offerings, New Year's decorations, children's toys. She burned them in a special furnace that reached a white heat where even memory turned to ash.

The smoke from her facility drifted over the Chinese City day and night. People began to forget not just the recent truths, but older things—traditional songs, family recipes, the proper way to fold a mourning dress. Shanghai was being unwritten.

Mei felt it in her own hands. She would reach for a fold she had known since childhood and find the memory slippery, as if coated in oil. The wind-folding became harder. The paper bird her father had made sang less clearly.

"We have to stop her," Mei told her mother.

"How do you stop someone who burns memory itself?"

Mei didn't know. So she went to the river.

The Huangpu was sluggish with silt and secrets. She stood at the Bund's edge, where foreign ships flew flags that had no business here, and she called to Chen Tian. Not with words—with paper. She folded a boat, whispered his name into its hull, and set it on the water. It sailed upstream against the current, defying physics.

Three days later, he answered. Not as a man—he was too dispersed for that—but as a gathering of water droplets that formed a face in her wash basin.

"Whitmore is not what she seems," the water-Chen said, his voice echoing from the porcelain. "She is a daughter of missionaries. She grew up here, in Shanghai. She learned our folding as a child, then betrayed it."

"Can she be reasoned with?"

"She thinks she is saving you," Chen Tian's watery face rippled with disgust. "She believes Chinese memory is diseased, superstitious. She wants to replace it with British order. Her furnace doesn't just burn paper—it *translates* it. Turns our grief into their progress."

"Then we give her something that cannot be translated."

Mei went to work. She didn't fold soldiers or seeds. She folded *ghosts*—paper figures of ancestors so complex, so saturated with authentic memory, that they could not be reduced to data. They were not messages. They were *people*, or as close as paper could come.

She used her father's tools, her aunt's techniques, and her own growing mastery. She folded her grandmother's memory of bound feet and liberation. She folded her father's cough, his death, his pride. She folded every moment of her own life that had made her Chinese—not in the political sense, but in the bone-deep way of belonging to a story that stretched back thousands of years.

These ghosts were not quiet. They wailed. They sang. They recited poetry in dialects so old even Mei didn't understand them. They were messy, contradictory, and utterly unburnable. Whitmore's furnace could reduce them to ash, but the ash would still remember. It would blow through the streets and into people's lungs, and the memories would take root again, stronger for having died once.

Mother helped, adding the White Lotus women's secret ingredient: blood. Not much—a pinprick on the finger, mixed with the ink. "Memory needs life to live," she said. "Your father was too pure. He forgot that we are not just minds. We are bodies. We bleed."

Mei bled into each ghost. It made them *hers* in a way nothing else had. She felt them attach to her, not as burdens, but as ancestors always should be—a presence, not a weight.

When she had fifty ghosts, she sent them into the city. Not hidden, but proudly, in the open. They marched through the streets during a British-sponsored parade celebrating some treaty or another. The foreign dignitaries saw them as traditional art, colorful natives showing their quaint customs. They didn't realize the ghosts were screaming.

Whitmore recognized them instantly. She tried to confiscate them, but the ghosts refused to be taken. They slipped through her agents' fingers like smoke. They appeared in her office, in her bedroom, in the furnace itself. They whispered to her in her sleep, not in Chinese, but in the English of her childhood, the dialect she had tried to forget: *"Do you remember the woman who taught you to fold, Miss Peregrine? Do you remember her name?"*

She had been taught by a nursemaid, Mei learned later. A woman from Guangdong who had folded paper animals to entertain the missionary child. Whitmore had loved her. Then, at age twelve, she had been sent back to England and told to forget such childish superstitions. She had complied, folding away her love for her teacher like a creased letter.

Now Mei's ghosts were unfolding it.

Whitmore's work faltered. She began making mistakes—burning the wrong papers, releasing half-formed memories into the air. The smoke from her furnace changed. It no longer smelled of pure forgetting. It smelled of regret.

Cartwright pulled her from the project. She was replaced by soldiers, by guns, by the blunt instruments of empire that had always lurked behind the civilized facade.

The paper soldiers were rounded up and burned publicly in the center of the French Concession. But the ghosts had already done their work. The truth was not in the soldiers anymore. It was in the people's lungs, in their blood, in the stories they told their children.

Mei watched the bonfire from across the river. She had not gone to see it; she didn't need to. She could feel each soldier's end as a small heat in her palm. They died as they were meant to: as martyrs, not secrets.

Her mother stood beside her, holding a paper lotus that glowed with a soft light. "The White Lotus women say this is only the beginning. The British will not leave because we remember. They will come harder."

"Let them," Mei said. "We have more paper."

But she was wrong. The next attack came not from the British, but from within.

Uncle Zhen found her. He appeared at the workshop door one morning in a Western suit that fit him too well, a British cigarette between his lips. He was not alone. Two warlord soldiers flanked him, their rifles held loosely—threateningly.

"The developer wants this property," Zhen said, as if no time had passed, as if they were still discussing a business transaction. "But more than that, he wants you, Mei. The paper girl who cost the British their best man."

Mei kept her hands steady, though her heart hammered. "I don't know what you mean."

"Don't insult me." Zhen threw a paper soldier onto the workbench. It was one of the originals, the ones Whitmore had burned. But it was whole, its ink fresh. "The British recovered this from a merchant in Suzhou. He coughed it up, literally. It had lodged in his lung. The thing had *grown* there, like a tumor of truth. Do you have any idea how dangerous that is? People are starting to believe they can breathe rebellion."

"Maybe they can."

"Maybe." Zhen's smile was cold. "But you won't be here to see it. The developer—his name is Sir Thomas Blackwood, by the way, newly knighted for his services to the Crown—has purchased you. You are to be his personal paper artist. You will create memories for him, pleasant ones. You will help him forget the unpleasant ones. And you will teach him how to fold the wind, so he can become invisible when the Chinese mob he has created finally turns on him."

The soldiers moved closer. Mei could smell their sweat, their gun oil, their complete indifference to the art on the walls around them.

Mother appeared from the back room, holding a teapot. "Stay for tea, brother?" she asked calmly.

Zhen snorted. "You always were too soft, sister. That's why you married a dreamer."

"Yes," Mother said. "And dreamers are harder to kill than you think."

She threw the teapot. Not at Zhen—at the wall. It shattered, and the hot water released a cloud of steam that was not steam. It was paper, unfurling, a thousand tiny figures that Mei had folded from her own blood and hidden in the walls for just such a moment.

They were not soldiers. They were not ghosts. They were *questions*—simple, unanswerable questions written in characters that rearranged themselves as you read them: *Who are you when you forget who sold you? What is the price of a memory? Can a nation be folded?*

The soldiers stopped, confused. These were not the revolutionary slogans they had been briefed on. These were philosophical riddles, the kind that keep a man awake at night. One soldier sat down, suddenly unable to remember why he was pointing a gun at his own people. The other began to weep.

Zhen pulled a revolver. "I am not a man who questions."

"But you are a man who forgets," Mei said. She had folded the wind around her, becoming a draft that slipped past his aim. "You forgot where you came from. You forgot who your family was. You forgot that paper can fold both ways."

She took the paper soldier from the table—the one he had thrown—and unfolded it. Not gently. She tore it, releasing its memory in a rush. It was the testimony of a worker, one of Blackwood's laborers who had died building his Shanghai tower. The memory was not of injustice, but of *pride*—the pride of a man who had built something that touched the sky.

Zhen saw it, and his face cracked. He had worked for Blackwood. He had overseen that tower's construction. He had known about the deaths, the corners cut, the bodies buried in concrete. He had folded that knowledge away.

Now it unfolded inside him.

The revolver clattered to the floor.

"Get out," Mother said to him, not unkindly. "And take your soldiers with you. They have families to remember."

Zhen left, but he took the paper soldier with him. Not as evidence, but as a reminder. Mei watched him go and knew he would never be fully free of it. The memory would itch like a splinter under the skin.

Sir Thomas Blackwood came himself the next week. He did not bring soldiers. He came alone, in a carriage with curtained windows, and he knocked on the workshop door like a gentleman.

Mei let him in. He was younger than she expected, with the smooth face of someone who had never done his own folding. In his hands was a small paper figure—a girl, elegantly dressed in the style of the Qing court.

"My grandmother," he said. "She lived here, before the Opium Wars. She taught me my first fold—a paper frog. I have not folded since."

"Why?"

"Because the frog jumped," he said simply. "And my father beat me for believing in Chinese magic. He sent me to Eton, where they taught me to fold nothing but napkins. I became a man who builds towers of glass and steel, but I have never built anything that *moves*."

He set the grandmother on the workbench. She was beautifully made, but her eyes were blank. She had no memory.

"I want you to teach me," Blackwood said. "Not to forget. To remember."

Mei studied him. This was a different kind of invasion—not with guns or furnaces, but with genuine need. He believed himself sincere. But his hands were clean, his nails manicured. He had not earned the right to remember.

"Your towers are built on bones," she said. "Fold them first."

He looked confused. "Fold bones?"

"Fold your guilt. Fold your crimes. Fold them until they are small enough to swallow, then choke on them." She pushed the grandmother toward him. "When you have done that, come back. Your hands will be stained enough to learn."

He left the figure behind. Mei held it, feeling its emptiness. It was a test, or a trap. She could fill it with a memory—perhaps the memory of the worker whose pride had cracked Zhen. Or she could leave it blank, a mirror of Blackwood's hollow soul.

She did something else. She folded it into a mirror, but a mirror that reflected not what was, but what could have been. When Blackwood looked into it, he would see the man he might have become if his father had not beaten the magic out of him. It was a cruel gift, but a necessary one.

The summer brought change, as summers do. The river carried Chen Tian's testimony to Nanjing, where a young officer read it and began to question his own orders. It reached Beijing, where a palace eunuch used it to blackmail a British diplomat. It even trickled into the countryside, where illiterate farmers heard it recited by traveling monks and understood, in their bones, that their suffering had a cause and a name.

Mei continued her quiet work. She made paper for weddings, for funerals, for the small ceremonies that hold a community together. But in each piece, she hid a question. Not a revolution—just a nudge. A reminder that memory is a choice.

Auntie Ying sent word that the Folded Ones were moving deeper into the mountains. "They are building a new valley," she wrote on paper that folded itself into a bird and flew to Mei's window. "A place where memory is the currency, and forgetting is the crime. You are invited."

Mei declined. Someone had to stay in Shanghai, to hold the line. The paper was here, in the streets, in the people. You cannot hide memory away in valleys. You must let it breathe the same dirty air as the oppressors.

Sofia wrote too, her letters more frequent now that she was free of her French name. "I have planted the soldiers in the most unlikely places. One is in the belly of a British opium ship. Another is in the mattress of a

missionary's daughter in Chengdu. I even sold one to a British officer's wife as a decorative fan. When she opens it on hot days, she will read about her husband's crimes in the language of perspiration."

The letter dissolved after reading, as all good letters should. Mei smiled at the thought of truth disguised as decoration. It was a very Chinese solution.

Her own work took a different turn. The ghosts she had made were effective, but they were also exhausting. Each one took a piece of her. The blood-folding was depleting her in ways she didn't understand until she woke one morning and couldn't remember the smell of her father's ink.

Panicked, she ran to the workshop. The paper bird still sang, but its voice was fainter. She realized that Whitmore's counter-memories had been more effective than she'd thought. They weren't just burning new memories—they were eroding old ones, the foundational ones that made her who she was.

She had been fighting a defensive war. It was time to attack.

The opportunity came from an unexpected source. Blackwood returned, but he was different. His hands were scarred now, his nails broken. He had been trying to fold, and failing.

"I cannot make the frog jump," he said, and there was a child's frustration in his voice. "I followed your instructions. I folded and refolded. But it remains paper."

"Show me," Mei said.

His fingers moved with painful slowness, creasing the paper into the familiar shape. But there was no breath in it, no memory of being alive. It was geometry, not art.

"You are folding with your mind," Mei said. "You must fold with your grief."

"I have no grief," he said automatically.

"Then you have nothing to fold." She took the frog from him, held it to her ear. "This paper was made from a mulberry tree that grew on a grave. The pulp remembers death. You must remember death too."

She told him to sit in the workshop for three days and three nights, folding nothing. Just sitting with the tools, the smell, the ghosts that still lingered. "Let the memories come," she said. "Even the ones you paid to have folded away."

He stayed. He did not eat. He did not sleep. On the third night, Mei found him weeping over a piece of paper, his tears staining it in patterns that became characters.

When she looked closer, she saw the characters were names. Workers who had died on his tower. The number was higher than she'd imagined.

"I remember them now," he whispered. "Each one. They are... they are *in* me."

"Good," Mei said. "Now fold."

He did. The frog he made was crooked, lopsided, but when he set it down, it hopped once. A small, clumsy hop. He laughed, a sound like a child discovering the world.

"I want to help," he said. "Tell me how."

Mei considered him. A man like Blackwood had access—papers, permits, the machinery of colonial power. He could smuggle memory in ways she could not. But he was also dangerous, untested. The frog could still become a trap.

"Build me a tower," she said finally. "But this time, build it with paper."

He looked confused. "A paper tower? It would collapse."

"Not if it is folded correctly. Not if it holds the memory of every tower that has ever fallen in China."

She gave him a design—her father's, found in a hidden notebook. It was a tower that folded upward instead of being built, a structure that existed in multiple states at once: blueprint, framework, and ruin. It would be simultaneously being constructed and being demolished, a perfect metaphor for colonialism itself.

"The British will see it as art," she explained. "Missionary groups will fund it as a symbol of East-West cooperation. And while they admire its cleverness, it will be doing what towers do best—falling."

Blackwood understood. The tower would be a machine for remembering failure. Every visitor who entered would experience, in small papercuts of memory, every tower the British had built on Chinese bones. They would forget their admiration. They would remember their guilt.

Construction began in the International Settlement, a place where no Chinese worker could officially lay a brick without foreign supervision. But Blackwood employed Chinese paper artists, paying them in silver that had been folded with memories of fair wages. The money felt heavier in their palms, and they spent it differently, as if it carried instruction.

The tower rose—not in stone, but in layers of pressed paper, each sheet containing a story. The foundation held the memory of the Old Summer Palace, burned by the British in 1860. The first floor recalled the railway stations built on seized land. The second floor remembered the churches erected where temples had been demolished. Each level was a wound, folded into a shape that looked like progress.

Whitmore came to inspect it. She stood at the base, her face closed, her hands folded in the British way—tight, controlled. "This is subversive," she said.

"It is a monument to cooperation," Blackwood replied, his voice smooth as a diplomat's. "See? The plaque." He gestured to a brass plate that described the tower as a symbol of Anglo-Chinese friendship.

Whitmore's fingers itched to unfold the lie, but the tower defied her. It was too honest in its dishonesty. It *knew* it was a paradox, and that made it immune to simple deconstruction.

She left, but she took a piece of the tower with her—a small paper scrap that had flaked off in the wind. Back at her facility, she placed it under her microscope and saw what Mei had done. The paper was alive with memories, but they were not angry. They were simply *present*. The memory of the burned palace did not demand revenge. It just asked to be seen.

For the first time, Whitmore understood the difference between preservation and propaganda. The British wanted to preserve their version of history—static, unchanging, dead. The Chinese paper artists preserved history as it truly was: alive, painful, and constantly re-folding itself.

She looked at her furnace, burning day and night, and saw it for what it was: a crematorium for truth. The smoke that rose from it did not purify. It only obscured.

That night, she did something unprecedented. She shut down the furnace. Then she began to fold.

Not with British precision, but with the clumsy, earnest desperation of a woman trying to remember a nursery rhyme from childhood. She folded a fan. On its ribs, she wrote every name she had helped erase. The fan would not close properly—grief had made it bulky—but when she snapped it open, it made a sound like a prayer.

She sent it to Mei, via a messenger boy who had no idea what he carried.

Mei opened it and smelled the smoke of the furnace mixed with the scent of old regret. The fan held Whitmore's apology, but more importantly, it held her *conversion*. She had folded her allegiance away.

The message was clear: the British were not monolithic. Some could be folded.

But others would double down.

Cartwright, increasingly desperate, convinced London to send more than paper artists. They sent soldiers. Real ones, with real guns, under orders to treat the memory epidemic as a literal disease. They began quarantining neighborhoods where paper art was practiced. They confiscated tools. They arrested anyone with ink-stained fingers.

Shanghai learned what it meant to be colonized in body, not just mind.

Mei's workshop was marked. A red X appeared on their door one morning, painted in ink that smelled of British authority. The neighboring houses were marked too—anyone who had ever bought a paper figure from the Li family.

Mother burned the mark off with a special paper flame, a trick from the White Lotus. "They will come tonight," she said. "We must be ready."

They were not ready for what came.

At midnight, the soldiers didn't knock. They unfolded through the walls, a British innovation—paper soldiers made from steel-hard parchment, animated by clockwork and cruelty. They had no memories, no souls. They were pure enforcement.

Mei's paper knife, her father's tool, could cut them, but they re-folded themselves. For every one she dismantled, two more appeared. They were not individuals, but a system, a bureaucracy given bodily form.

Mother was cornered. One of the soldiers pressed a paper hand to her forehead, and she gasped as her memories began to drain—her marriage, her daughter's birth, her husband's face. It was being *downloaded* into a paper scroll in the soldier's chest.

Mei did the only thing she could. She folded herself into the soldier.

It was like diving into ice water. The soldier's interior was a labyrinth of forms, regulations, lists of things to be erased. She moved through it as wind, searching for her mother's memory. She found it wrapped in a tight scroll, already half-transcribed.

She unfolded it, but gently, the way you would open a letter from a lover. The memory flowed back into Mother, but Mei kept a copy, folded small in her palm.

Ejecting herself from the soldier was harder. Its system recognized her as an anomaly, a Chinese virus in British code. It began to fold around her, trying to trap her in paperwork, in endless reports and requisitions.

She remembered her father's final lesson: *Death is just another kind of folding.*

She folded herself into death. Not her own, but the memory of every death these soldiers had caused. She became a ghost inside the machine, haunting it with the weight of Chinese grief.

The soldier collapsed, its clockwork jammed by sorrow it could not process. The others froze, their systems overloaded by the sudden presence of empathy.

Mei reformed, gasping, holding her mother's memory like a wounded bird. Mother was unconscious but breathing, her face peaceful.

The workshop was in ruins. Paper scraps everywhere, each one a potential soldier, a potential ghost. Mei gathered them, stuffing them into her satchel. She didn't have time to be careful.

From the window, she saw more mechanical soldiers unfolding down the street, a paper army of oblivion. The British had decided that if they could not control Chinese memory, they would replace it with their own sterile version.

She had to warn the city.

But she was one girl with a satchel of scraps. Even the wind-folding couldn't make her invisible to an army that had no eyes to see, only regulations to follow.

Then Blackwood appeared in the alley behind the workshop. His motorcar idled, its engine running. "Get in," he said. "I know where we can hide the paper."

He took them to his tower. It was almost complete, a spiraling construction of living paper that looked like a tornado frozen mid-swirl. Inside, it was hollow—a vast space where echoes lived.

"The soldiers cannot enter," Blackwood explained. "The tower is technically British property, but it is also anti-British art. Their programming cannot resolve the paradox. They circle it, but they cannot cross the threshold."

He set Mother in a room at the tower's heart, where paper lanterns pulsed with soft light. "She will be safe here. The tower remembers all who enter. It will not forget her."

Mei looked at him—this man who had tried to buy her, who was now sheltering her. "Why?"

"Because you were right. I had to fold my grief to understand it. Now I have to unfold my privilege to atone for it." He gestured to the tower's walls. "This is not my redemption. It is my testimony. When it is finished, it will collapse in a very specific way—folding inward, becoming a book. A book of every crime I profited from."

He would die with it, Mei realized. He would be folded into the final chapter.

"Don't," she said. "We need living witnesses, not more dead martyrs."

But he was already folding, his hands moving with the grace of a man who had finally found the right shape for his life. The tower began to hum, its paper walls vibrating with anticipation.

Outside, the mechanical soldiers had surrounded the base. They were drilling, trying to find a legal loophole that would let them enter. They had time. Not much, but enough.

Mei climbed to the tower's top, carrying her satchel. From here, she could see Shanghai spread out like a map—foreign concessions glowing with electric light, Chinese City dark but alive with cooking fires. She could see the smoke from Whitmore's silent furnace. She could see the river, carrying its cargo of memory to the sea.

She opened the satchel and let the paper scraps fly. They were not organized, not soldiers or ghosts. They were chaos, the raw material of memory. Each scrap held a fragment: a recipe, a song, a curse, a blessing, the proper way to hold a child, the correct angle for mourning.

They drifted down on the mechanical soldiers like snow. The soldiers tried to fold them away, but you cannot file chaos. The scraps lodged in their joints, in their gears, in the empty spaces where memories should have been. They began to malfunction, not from damage, but from *confusion*. A soldier that has never remembered cannot process the sudden influx of a thousand ordinary lives.

One by one, they folded themselves into stillness, becoming sculptures of bureaucratic failure.

Mei watched them fall and felt not triumph, but exhaustion. This was not victory. It was simply maintenance. The British would send more. They would send worse. The war of memory would not end with paper.

It would end when the people no longer needed paper to remember.

She climbed down. Blackwood was gone, folded into the tower's walls. Mother was awake, her eyes clear. Together, they left the tower, stepping over the frozen soldiers.

Behind them, the tower began its final collapse. It did not fall. It *folded*, each level tucking into the one below, becoming smaller and denser until it was the size of a book, then a pamphlet, then a single sheet of paper so fine it could only be read by the light of the moon on a particular night in midsummer.

Mei caught it as it drifted down. It held Blackwood's confession, his crimes, his tower, his death. But it also held something else: the memory of the day he had folded his first frog, and it had hopped. The moment before his father beat him. The moment when he was still innocent.

She folded that memory small and pressed it into the palm of a beggar child on the street. "Plant this," she whispered. "It will grow into something that cannot be built."

The winter came, and with it, a strange peace. The British, confused by their failure and nervous about the spreading memory-disease, pulled back. They fortified their concessions and left the Chinese City to its own devices. It was not freedom, but it was breathing room.

Mei reopened the workshop. Not as a secret revolutionary cell, but as what it had always been—a place where paper was made for the living and the dead. She taught her art to anyone who came, but she taught it truly. Each student had to fold their own grief first. The workshop became a place of strange pilgrimage—people came with loss and left with skill.

Some became soldiers in the memory war. Others simply became people who could preserve their own stories. That was enough.

One morning, a letter arrived on a paper crane. It was from Chen Tian, but he was not a soldier anymore. He was a river, and the river was everywhere.

"The British are negotiating a withdrawal," the letter said, the characters dissolving and reforming as she read. "Not from all of China—never that—but from Shanghai. The truth has made the city ungovernable. They cannot erase what we have unfolded."

The letter ended with a question: *What will you do when the war is over?*

Mei looked around the workshop—her mother teaching a young girl the proper way to hold a brush, a British expatriate struggling to fold his guilt, a stack of fresh paper waiting to become something more than pulp.

She wrote her answer on the crane's wing, and it flew away: *I will fold the peace.*

The crane carried it to the river, to the mountains, to every place where memory had taken root. It was not an ending. It was a continuation—the work her father had started, now unfolded into a future he could not have imagined.

In the workshop, she picked up her father's knife and began a new figure. Not a soldier, not a ghost, but a gardener. A woman with her own face, holding a paper seed.

The paper soldiers worked better than they could have imagined—and that was the problem.

Within a month of Mei's return to Shanghai, the British Concession was in crisis. Hawthorne's replacement, a pinch-faced man named Cartwright, found his desk covered in paper effigies each morning—tiny soldiers that whispered bank account numbers when he tried to sleep. The French reported similar occurrences: paper boats clogging their harbor, each one inscribed with details of missionary gun-running. In the Chinese City, warlord troops confiscated hundreds of the figures, only to have their own men start reciting the testimony, word for word, in their sleep.

The truth, once folded, could not be contained. It had a life of its own.

Mei heard these reports from her workshop window, where she now made only neutral things—paper flowers for festivals, generic ancestors for families who couldn't afford better. The real work happened at night, in the hidden room beneath the floorboards where her father had once stored his tools. There, she crafted the next generation of memory keepers: not soldiers, but seeds. Paper dandelions that would drift on the wind, carrying whispers of rebellion. Paper rice grains that would hide in sacks, passing through countless hands before releasing their stories.

Her mother, once practical, now helped with the folding. The White Lotus women had taught her their own paper arts—how to make lotus blossoms that could hide a woman from soldiers' eyes, how to fold prayers so small they could be swallowed if searched. She brought these techniques home like contraband wisdom.

"You are making them desperate," Mother warned one evening, watching Mei fold a particularly delicate rice grain. "Desperate men do desperate things."

She was right.

The retaliation came on the fifth of the month, during the Qingming Festival when families burned offerings for their dead. This year, the smoke over Shanghai smelled wrong—acrid, chemical. People who inhaled it fell sick, their memories fracturing. They forgot their children's names, their own addresses, the faces of their ancestors. The British claimed it was a new strain of influenza, but the paper artists knew better.

They were burning *counter-memories*.

Cartwright had brought in a new expert—a woman this time, which surprised Mei. Miss Peregrine Whitmore looked like a governess from a Dickens novel: stern bun, sharper eyes, fingers that moved with the clinical precision of a surgeon. She didn't fold paper. She *unfolded* it, strip by strip, until nothing remained but pulp and regret.

"They call her the Librarian," Auntie Ying's message said, delivered by a paper moth that dissolved after speaking. "She works for the British Museum, cataloguing colonized memories. She has a furnace that burns at the temperature of forgetting."

Whitmore's method was surgical and precise. She targeted the paper soldiers one by one, tracing them through the supply chains and confiscating them. But she didn't stop there. She began collecting *all* paper art—genuine funeral offerings, New Year's decorations, children's toys. She burned them in a special furnace that reached a white heat where even memory turned to ash.

The smoke from her facility drifted over the Chinese City day and night. People began to forget not just the recent truths, but older things—traditional songs, family recipes, the proper way to fold a mourning dress. Shanghai was being unwritten.

Mei felt it in her own hands. She would reach for a fold she had known since childhood and find the memory slippery, as if coated in oil. The wind-folding became harder. The paper bird her father had made sang less clearly.

"We have to stop her," Mei told her mother.

"How do you stop someone who burns memory itself?"

Mei didn't know. So she went to the river.

The Huangpu was sluggish with silt and secrets. She stood at the Bund's edge, where foreign ships flew flags that had no business here, and she called to Chen Tian. Not with words—with paper. She folded a boat, whispered his name into its hull, and set it on the water. It sailed upstream against the current, defying physics.

Three days later, he answered. Not as a man—he was too dispersed for that—but as a gathering of water droplets that formed a face in her wash basin.

"Whitmore is not what she seems," the water-Chen said, his voice echoing from the porcelain. "She is a daughter of missionaries. She grew up here, in Shanghai. She learned our folding as a child, then betrayed it."

"Can she be reasoned with?"

"She thinks she is saving you," Chen Tian's watery face rippled with disgust. "She believes Chinese memory is diseased, superstitious. She wants to replace it with British order. Her furnace doesn't just burn paper—it *translates* it. Turns our grief into their progress."

"Then we give her something that cannot be translated."

Mei went to work. She didn't fold soldiers or seeds. She folded *ghosts*—paper figures of ancestors so complex, so saturated with authentic memory, that they could not be reduced to data. They were not messages. They were *people*, or as close as paper could come.

She used her father's tools, her aunt's techniques, and her own growing mastery. She folded her grandmother's memory of bound feet and liberation. She folded her father's cough, his death, his pride. She folded every moment of her own life that had made her Chinese—not in the political sense, but in the bone-deep way of belonging to a story that stretched back thousands of years.

These ghosts were not quiet. They wailed. They sang. They recited poetry in dialects so old even Mei didn't understand them. They were messy, contradictory, and utterly unburnable. Whitmore's furnace could reduce them to ash, but the ash would still remember. It would blow through the streets and into people's lungs, and the memories would take root again, stronger for having died once.

Mother helped, adding the White Lotus women's secret ingredient: blood. Not much—a pinprick on the finger, mixed with the ink. "Memory needs life to live," she said. "Your father was too pure. He forgot that we are not just minds. We are bodies. We bleed."

Mei bled into each ghost. It made them *hers* in a way nothing else had. She felt them attach to her, not as burdens, but as ancestors always should be—a presence, not a weight.

When she had fifty ghosts, she sent them into the city. Not hidden, but proudly, in the open. They marched through the streets during a British-sponsored parade celebrating some treaty or another. The foreign dignitaries saw them as traditional art, colorful natives showing their quaint customs. They didn't realize the ghosts were screaming.

Whitmore recognized them instantly. She tried to confiscate them, but the ghosts refused to be taken. They slipped through her agents' fingers like smoke. They appeared in her office, in her bedroom, in the furnace itself. They whispered to her in her sleep, not in Chinese, but in the English of her childhood, the dialect she had tried to forget: *"Do you remember the woman who taught you to fold, Miss Peregrine? Do you remember her name?"*

She had been taught by a nursemaid, Mei learned later. A woman from Guangdong who had folded paper animals to entertain the missionary child. Whitmore had loved her. Then, at age twelve, she had been sent back to England and told to forget such childish superstitions. She had complied, folding away her love for her teacher like a creased letter.

Now Mei's ghosts were unfolding it.

Whitmore's work faltered. She began making mistakes—burning the wrong papers, releasing half-formed memories into the air. The smoke from her furnace changed. It no longer smelled of pure forgetting. It smelled of regret.

Cartwright pulled her from the project. She was replaced by soldiers, by guns, by the blunt instruments of empire that had always lurked behind the civilized facade.

The paper soldiers were rounded up and burned publicly in the center of the French Concession. But the ghosts had already done their work. The truth was not in the soldiers anymore. It was in the people's lungs, in their blood, in the stories they told their children.

Mei watched the bonfire from across the river. She had not gone to see it; she didn't need to. She could feel each soldier's end as a small heat in her palm. They died as they were meant to: as martyrs, not secrets.

Her mother stood beside her, holding a paper lotus that glowed with a soft light. "The White Lotus women say this is only the beginning. The British will not leave because we remember. They will come harder."

"Let them," Mei said. "We have more paper."

But she was wrong. The next attack came not from the British, but from within.

Uncle Zhen found her. He appeared at the workshop door one morning in a Western suit that fit him too well, a British cigarette between his lips. He was not alone. Two warlord soldiers flanked him, their rifles held loosely—threateningly.

"The developer wants this property," Zhen said, as if no time had passed, as if they were still discussing a business transaction. "But more than that, he wants you, Mei. The paper girl who cost the British their best man."

Mei kept her hands steady, though her heart hammered. "I don't know what you mean."

"Don't insult me." Zhen threw a paper soldier onto the workbench. It was one of the originals, the ones Whitmore had burned. But it was whole, its ink fresh. "The British recovered this from a merchant in Suzhou. He coughed it up, literally. It had lodged in his lung. The thing had *grown* there, like a tumor of truth. Do you have any idea how dangerous that is? People are starting to believe they can breathe rebellion."

"Maybe they can."

"Maybe." Zhen's smile was cold. "But you won't be here to see it. The developer—his name is Sir Thomas Blackwood, by the way, newly knighted for his services to the Crown—has purchased you. You are to be his personal paper artist. You will create memories for him, pleasant ones. You will help him forget the unpleasant ones. And you will teach him how to fold the wind, so he can become invisible when the Chinese mob he has created finally turns on him."

The soldiers moved closer. Mei could smell their sweat, their gun oil, their complete indifference to the art on the walls around them.

Mother appeared from the back room, holding a teapot. "Stay for tea, brother?" she asked calmly.

Zhen snorted. "You always were too soft, sister. That's why you married a dreamer."

"Yes," Mother said. "And dreamers are harder to kill than you think."

She threw the teapot. Not at Zhen—at the wall. It shattered, and the hot water released a cloud of steam that was not steam. It was paper, unfurling, a thousand tiny figures that Mei had folded from her own blood and hidden in the walls for just such a moment.

They were not soldiers. They were not ghosts. They were *questions*—simple, unanswerable questions written in characters that rearranged themselves as you read them: *Who are you when you forget who sold you? What is the price of a memory? Can a nation be folded?*

The soldiers stopped, confused. These were not the revolutionary slogans they had been briefed on. These were philosophical riddles, the kind that keep a man awake at night. One soldier sat down, suddenly unable to remember why he was pointing a gun at his own people. The other began to weep.

Zhen pulled a revolver. "I am not a man who questions."

"But you are a man who forgets," Mei said. She had folded the wind around her, becoming a draft that slipped past his aim. "You forgot where you came from. You forgot who your family was. You forgot that paper can fold both ways."

She took the paper soldier from the table—the one he had thrown—and unfolded it. Not gently. She tore it, releasing its memory in a rush. It was the testimony of a worker, one of Blackwood's laborers who had died building his Shanghai tower. The memory was not of injustice, but of *pride*—the pride of a man who had built something that touched the sky.

Zhen saw it, and his face cracked. He had worked for Blackwood. He had overseen that tower's construction. He had known about the deaths, the corners cut, the bodies buried in concrete. He had folded that knowledge away.

Now it unfolded inside him.

The revolver clattered to the floor.

"Get out," Mother said to him, not unkindly. "And take your soldiers with you. They have families to remember."

Zhen left, but he took the paper soldier with him. Not as evidence, but as a reminder. Mei watched him go and knew he would never be fully free of it. The memory would itch like a splinter under the skin.

Sir Thomas Blackwood came himself the next week. He did not bring soldiers. He came alone, in a carriage with curtained windows, and he knocked on the workshop door like a gentleman.

Mei let him in. He was younger than she expected, with the smooth face of someone who had never done his own folding. In his hands was a small paper figure—a girl, elegantly dressed in the style of the Qing court.

"My grandmother," he said. "She lived here, before the Opium Wars. She taught me my first fold—a paper frog. I have not folded since."

"Why?"

"Because the frog jumped," he said simply. "And my father beat me for believing in Chinese magic. He sent me to Eton, where they taught me to fold nothing but napkins. I became a man who builds towers of glass and steel, but I have never built anything that *moves*."

"I want you to teach me," Blackwood said, his voice barely a whisper. "Not to forget. To remember."

Mei studied him. This was a different kind of invasion—not with guns or furnaces, but with genuine need. He believed himself sincere. But his hands were clean, his nails manicured. He had not earned the right to remember.

"Your towers are built on bones," she said. "Fold them first."

He looked confused. "Fold bones?"

"Fold your guilt. Fold your crimes. Fold them until they are small enough to swallow, then choke on them." She pushed the grandmother toward him. "When you have done that, come back. Your hands will be stained enough to learn."

He left the figure behind. Mei held it, feeling its emptiness. It was a test, or a trap. She could fill it with a memory—perhaps the memory of the worker whose pride had cracked Zhen. Or she could leave it blank, a mirror of Blackwood's hollow soul.

She did something else. She folded it into a mirror, but a mirror that reflected not what was, but what could have been. When Blackwood looked into it, he would see the man he might have become if his father had not beaten the magic out of him. It was a cruel gift, but a necessary one.

The summer brought change, as summers do. The river carried Chen Tian's testimony to Nanjing, where a young officer read it and began to question his own orders. It reached Beijing, where a palace eunuch used it to blackmail a British diplomat. It even trickled into the countryside, where illiterate farmers heard it recited by traveling monks and understood, in their bones, that their suffering had a cause and a name.

Mei continued her quiet work. She made paper for weddings, for funerals, for the small ceremonies that hold a community together. But in each piece, she hid a question. Not a revolution—just a nudge. A reminder that memory is a choice.

Auntie Ying sent word that the Folded Ones were moving deeper into the mountains. "They are building a new valley," she wrote on paper that folded itself into a bird and flew to Mei's window. "A place where memory is the currency, and forgetting is the crime. You are invited."

Mei declined. Someone had to stay in Shanghai, to hold the line. The paper was here, in the streets, in the people. You cannot hide memory away in valleys. You must let it breathe the same dirty air as the oppressors.

Sofia wrote too, her letters more frequent now that she was free of her French name. "I have planted the soldiers in the most unlikely places. One is in the belly of a British opium ship. Another is in the mattress of a missionary's daughter in Chengdu. I even sold one to a British officer's wife as a decorative fan. When she opens it on hot days, she will read about her husband's crimes in the language of perspiration."

The letter dissolved after reading, as all good letters should. Mei smiled at the thought of truth disguised as decoration. It was a very Chinese solution.

Her own work took a different turn. The ghosts she had made were effective, but they were also exhausting. Each one took a piece of her. The blood-folding was depleting her in ways she didn't understand until she woke one morning and couldn't remember the smell of her father's ink.

Panicked, she ran to the workshop. The paper bird still sang, but its voice was fainter. The counter-memories had been eroding her foundations—she'd been so focused on planting new truths that she'd neglected to protect her own.

She pulled out her father's hidden notebook, the one that had started everything. Inside, on a page she'd never noticed before, was a single phrase in his handwriting: *To preserve others, you must first preserve yourself.*

Below it was a design for a paper *self*—a figure that could hold your own memories, a backup soul. It was the most complex fold she'd ever seen, requiring 108 precise creases, each representing a year of a long life. Her father had never made it; his lungs had given out first.

Mei began to fold. She used her own blood, her own hair—one strand, plucked and split into threads for binding. She folded her earliest memory: her mother's hands kneading dough, the dough becoming paper, the paper becoming a world. She folded her father's cough, his death, his pride. She folded the smell of camphor and the taste of grief.

The figure was small, no larger than her palm. It had her face, but younger, untouched by the war. When it was done, she placed it in a silk pouch and wore it around her neck.

Immediately, she felt stronger. The bird's song grew clearer. Her hands steadied. She had made herself a memory-anchor, a paper twin that could hold her essence while she gave the rest away.

But the act of creation had attracted attention. The British had been hunting for the source of the paper soldiers, and now they had a signature—blood magic, the one thing their machines couldn't replicate. Whitmore might have been converted, but Cartwright was still hunting. And he had a new tool: a paper net, woven from transcripts of forgotten trials, that could catch anything made with human essence.

The net found her on a Tuesday.

She was buying rice in the market when the air grew thick with the smell of old verdicts. The net descended, invisible but absolute, and she felt the paper self around her neck grow hot, then cold, then numb. It was being separated from her, caught.

She ran, leaving the rice, but the net followed. It was not a physical thing; it was a legal construct, a bureaucratic spell that declared certain memories *contraband*. And right now, it had decided that Mei Li was contraband.

She ducked into an alley, into a teahouse, into the maze of the Chinese City. The net followed, moving through walls, through people, through time itself. It was going to catch her, and when it did, it would unfold her, strip by strip, until it found the source of the soldiers.

Then she remembered: the net was made of paper. And paper could be folded.

She stopped running. She turned to face the net, which hung in the air behind her like a spider's web made of invisible ink. She reached out and touched it.

The net was cold, brittle. It hummed with legal language, with the authority of empire. But it was also *old*, woven from precedents set in the 17th century, from laws that had been folded so many times they were beginning to tear at the creases.

Mei folded the net.

It was the hardest thing she had ever done. She had to fold while it tried to unfold her. She had to crease it along lines of logic it didn't know it had. She folded it into a shape that was both a cage and a key, a paradox that made it fold back on itself.

When she was done, the net was a small paper puzzle in her palm. It would hold itself for a while, but eventually it would try to unfold. When it did, it would release all the memories it had ever caught—not just hers, but every contraband thought, every suppressed story, every erased name from a hundred colonies.

She pocketed it and walked home.

Cartwright was waiting at the workshop, his face red with fury. "You have something that belongs to the Crown," he said.

"I have many things that belong to the Crown," Mei replied. "Including its conscience."

She threw the puzzle at his feet. It unfolded in an instant, not into a net, but into a flock of paper birds—each one a memory the British had tried to erase from some distant shore. They rose in a cloud, shrieking with borrowed voices, and flew toward the Bund.

Cartwright screamed orders, but his soldiers couldn't shoot down memories. The birds settled on the British consulate, covering it in a living wallpaper of testimony. Passersby stopped to read: stories from India, from Africa, from Ireland, from everywhere the British had tried to fold history to their will.

It was a diaspora of the forgotten.

Cartwright turned on Mei, his hand raised to strike. But Mother stepped between them, holding a paper lotus that bloomed in his face. The petals held his own mother's face, her voice scolding him for being cruel to the servants. He faltered.

"Get out," Mother said, and this time it was not a suggestion. "Take your machines. Take your nets. Shanghai does not need you."

He left, but he took the furnace with him. Not Whitmore's silent furnace, but a new one, mobile, mounted on the back of a truck. He would take his war of forgetting on the road.

That night, Mei dreamed of her father again. This time, he was not alone. He stood in a field of paper flowers, each one a person who had died for memory. Chen Tian was there, and Blackwood's worker, and even Whitmore's nursemaid, folding frogs for a child who would one day betray her.

"Mei," her father said, "the time has come to choose. You can fold the past into a weapon, or you can unfold the future into a garden. But you cannot do both."

"Which did you choose?" she asked.

"I chose the weapon," he admitted. "That is why I died. The weapon cut me as I wielded it."

"What should I choose?"

"Choose the garden," he said. "But plant it in their courts. Let it grow through the floorboards of their power. Let it bloom in the cracks of their certainty."

She woke with a plan.

The next morning, she went to the British consulate. Not as a revolutionary, but as a merchant's daughter with a basket of funeral offerings. She requested an audience with Cartwright, claiming she had come to negotiate the surrender of the remaining paper soldiers.

He received her in his office, the walls still covered in the birds she had released. They had settled into a mural of silent testimony. He looked exhausted, as if the memories had been keeping him awake.

"I will give you the soldiers," Mei said, "if you give me the furnace."

He laughed. "The furnace is Crown property. It cost a fortune."

"The soldiers are Chinese property," she countered. "They cost more."

They haggled. It was a very Shanghai negotiation, full of tea and cigarettes and the pretense of civility. In the end, he agreed to a temporary loan—one week with the furnace, in exchange for fifty soldiers she had "hidden" in the city.

The soldiers were real, but they were decoys. Each one held a single, repetitive memory: the sound of a frog hopping. It was not a lie, but it was not the truth they wanted. Cartwright would get his soldiers, and they would drive him mad with their simplicity.

The furnace arrived at the workshop on a flatbed truck, its iron belly still warm. It was an ugly thing, squat and functional, designed by engineers who had never touched rice paper.

"Now what?" Mother asked.

"We turn it on," Mei said, "and we burn everything."

They burned the counter-memories. The chemical smoke that had made people forget. They burned the mechanical soldiers, folding them into the flames so their clockwork sang a final, mournful song. They burned the transcripts of forgotten trials, the legal nets, the bureaucratic forms.

The smoke rose black and thick, but it smelled... clean. Like renewal. Like the workshop after a long night's work.

Then Mei did something the British never would have allowed. She burned her own paper self.

The duplicate figure went into the furnace with a whisper. It held her fear, her exhaustion, her grief. It held the parts of her that had been depleted by the blood-folding. As it burned, she felt those things leave her—not forgotten, but *transmuted*. The fear became courage. The exhaustion became peace. The grief became love.

The furnace, designed to erase, had been turned into a crucible for transformation.

When Cartwright returned, furious at the decoy soldiers, he found the workshop empty and the furnace cold. Its iron sides had been folded into a new shape—a lotus flower six feet tall, its petals inscribed with the names of every person whose memory it had been used to erase.

Mei was gone, but she had left a message, folded from the smoke itself. It hung in the air, visible only when the light hit it just right: *We do not forget. We cannot be erased. We are the paper you write your history on.*

Cartwright had the lotus dismantled and shipped back to London. It was studied by experts, declared an unsolvable anomaly, and eventually stored in the British Museum's basement, where it sat next to the Elgin Marbles and other stolen memories.

Mei, meanwhile, had taken the real soldiers—fifty figures, each dense with truth—to the river. She set them on the water in a fleet of paper boats, each one smaller than a leaf. They sailed downstream, not as an army, but as a diaspora.

Chen Tian's voice rose from the water: *"You have given them freedom. Now they will choose what to remember."*

She watched them go, then turned to face her city. Shanghai was changed. Not free, not yet, but awake. In the alleys, people were folding again—not for rebellion, but for themselves. In the teahouses, the paper birds sang. In the bedrooms, lovers hid paper hearts beneath their pillows.

Auntie Ying's final message arrived on a paper crane that would not dissolve. It was made from something more permanent, something that had been folded so many times it had become metal.

"The war of memory is over," it said. "The work of memory has just begun. Come home when you can."

The crane flew away, and Mei understood. She had been fighting a war, but her father had been waging a revolution. War ends. Revolutions endure.

She reopened the workshop the next day, but she changed the sign. No longer "Li Family Funeral Offerings." Now it read, simply: *Li Mei, Memory Keeper.*

People came. Not just the bereaved, but the hopeful, the confused, the angry. They brought stories. She folded them. Some she sent to the mountains, to Auntie Ying's new valley. Some she sent downriver, to join Chen Tian's endless testimony. Some she kept, building a library of the living, a counterweight to the British Museum's basement.

Whitmore came, too, her governess's bun loosened, her hands stained with ink. She brought her fan, the one that wouldn't close, and asked Mei to teach her the proper way to fold a frog.

They sat together, the colonizer and the colonized, folding paper that had been made from mulberry trees on graves. The frog that Whitmore made was crooked, but it hopped. When it landed, it dissolved into characters that spelled a single word in both English and Chinese: *Remember*.

Years passed. The British did not leave Shanghai, but they stopped burning memories. They learned, as all empires eventually do, that you cannot govern people whose stories you do not control. They retreated to their concessions, and the Chinese City grew around them, a jungle of paper and purpose.

Mei married no one. She had no children. But she had students, hundreds of them, each one a living memory of her father's art. And she had the paper twin, the one she had burned in the furnace, which had not been destroyed but *released*. It sometimes visited her in dreams, a younger self who reminded her to be afraid, to be tired, to be human.

On her thirtieth birthday, a paper boat sailed up the Huangpu against the current and stopped at the workshop door. Inside was a figure of a woman, painted with exquisite detail, holding a jade comb.

It was her grandmother. The one from her dream. The one who had said, *"He must remember. He must."*

Mei placed it on the workbench, next to her father's tools. The grandmother's eyes moved, just slightly, and Mei understood. The "he" was not her father. It was not Chen Tian. It was Mei herself.

*He must remember* meant *She must remember*. The pronoun was a fold in time, a trick of the light.

She must remember that she was not just a weapon, not just a garden, but both. She must remember that every fold contains its opposite, that every memory holds its own erasure, that every revolution ends with someone sweeping up the paper scraps.

She folded a crane. Not for war, not for peace, but for the simple pleasure of seeing paper become bird. It flew out the window, not toward the mountains or the river, but up, into the Shanghai

sky, into the haze of factory smoke and cooking fires that hung over the city like a memory of itself. It didn't try to escape. It flew in circles, each loop tracing a character in the air: *Remember. Remember. Remember.*

Mei watched it until it dissolved into the evening, becoming just another speck of ash in a city that breathed smoke. She turned back to the workshop and found her mother watching her with eyes that had seen two revolutions and were waiting for a third.

"You've stopped fighting," Mother observed, not as a criticism but as a statement of fact.

"I've started building," Mei corrected. "Fighting is what you do when you think you can win. Building is what you do when you know you cannot lose."

The workshop had changed while she wasn't looking. The hidden room beneath the floorboards had expanded, its walls now lined with paper figures that seemed to shift when she wasn't watching. The paper bird her father made had stopped singing—not because it was dying, but because it had learned silence, the kind that comes before dawn.
